Bell States

$$|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)$$

The four Bell states are maximally entangled two-qubit states. They form a complete orthonormal basis for the two-qubit Hilbert space.

Density Matrix

$$\rho = |\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+|$$

For a pure Bell state, the $4\times4$ density matrix has off-diagonal coherences. Decoherence destroys these, driving the state toward the maximally mixed state $\rho = \mathbf{I}/4$.

Decoherence

$$\rho(t) = (1-p)\,|\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+| + p\,\frac{\mathbf{I}}{4}$$

The parameter $p$ interpolates between a pure entangled state ($p=0$) and white noise ($p=1$).

Channel Fidelity

$$F = \langle\Phi^+|\rho|\Phi^+\rangle = 1 - \frac{3p}{4}$$

Fidelity measures how close the transmitted state is to the ideal Bell state. Quantum key distribution requires $F > 0.5$.

Entanglement Entropy

$$S = -\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_A \ln\rho_A) = \ln 2$$

A maximally entangled pair has one ebit of entanglement. Measuring one qubit instantly collapses the other — regardless of distance.

Micius Satellite (2017)

Pan Jianwei's team launched the Micius satellite at ~500 km altitude and distributed entangled photon pairs to ground stations at Delingha and Lijiang, separated by ~1200 km.

CHSH Inequality

$$S = |E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')| \leq 2$$

Classical correlations satisfy $S \leq 2$. Quantum mechanics predicts a maximum of $2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.83$. Micius measured $S = 2.37 \pm 0.09$, a clear violation.

Loss Comparison

$$\text{Fiber: }0.2\;\mathrm{dB/km}\times 1200\;\mathrm{km} = 240\;\mathrm{dB}$$
$$\text{Satellite: }\sim 27\;\mathrm{dB}\text{ (geometric spreading)}$$

Through 1200 km of fiber, essentially every photon is lost ($10^{-24}$ transmission). The satellite route has orders of magnitude less loss — vacuum is nearly transparent.

Photon Source

A BBO crystal on-board Micius generates entangled photon pairs via spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC). One photon is sent to each ground station simultaneously.

Nature 549, 43–47 (2017) — Yin et al.

Quantum Teleportation

Alice can transmit an arbitrary qubit state $|\psi\rangle$ to Bob using one pre-shared Bell pair and two classical bits — without the physical qubit ever traveling.

The Protocol

$$|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$$

Step 1 — Bell Measurement: Alice measures her two qubits (the unknown state and her half of the Bell pair) in the Bell basis, collapsing to one of four outcomes.

Step 2 — Classical Communication: Alice sends her 2-bit measurement result to Bob via a classical channel.

Step 3 — Correction: Bob applies one of $\{I, X, Z, XZ\}$ to his qubit, reconstructing $|\psi\rangle$.

No FTL Communication

$$\rho_B = \mathrm{Tr}_A(\rho) = \frac{I}{2}$$

Before receiving the classical bits, Bob's qubit is maximally mixed — he gains zero information. The classical channel (speed $\leq c$) is essential.

No-Cloning Theorem

Teleportation does not copy the state — Alice's original is destroyed in the Bell measurement. This is required by the no-cloning theorem.

Bennett et al., PRL 70, 1895 (1993)

Quantum Repeaters

Photon loss in fiber scales exponentially with distance. Without repeaters, the maximum range is determined by $L_{\mathrm{att}} \approx 20\,\mathrm{km}$ (attenuation length).

Without Repeaters

$$P_{\mathrm{direct}}(L) = e^{-L/L_{\mathrm{att}}}$$

At 1000 km this gives $P \approx e^{-50} \approx 10^{-22}$ — completely infeasible.

Entanglement Swapping

$$|\Phi^+\rangle_{AR_1}\otimes|\Phi^+\rangle_{R_1B} \xrightarrow{\text{BSM at }R_1} |\Phi^+\rangle_{AB}$$

A Bell State Measurement (BSM) at repeater node $R_1$ entangles Alice and Bob, consuming both short-range pairs.

With $n$ Segments

$$P_n \propto \left(\frac{L_{\mathrm{att}}}{L/n}\right)^{-n} = \left(\frac{n}{L/L_{\mathrm{att}}}\right)^n$$

The rate still decreases with total distance, but polynomially rather than exponentially — a dramatic improvement.

Quantum Memory

Repeaters require quantum memories to hold entanglement while waiting for neighbouring links to succeed. Atomic ensembles, nitrogen-vacancy centres, and trapped ions are leading candidates.

Briegel et al., PRL 81, 5932 (1998)

BB84 Protocol (Bennett & Brassard 1984)

Alice encodes random bits in two bases: Z ($|0\rangle,|1\rangle$) or X ($|+\rangle,|-\rangle$). Bob measures in a random basis. They publicly compare bases and keep only matching rounds.

$$\text{QBER} = \frac{\text{errors}}{\text{sifted bits}}$$

Security Threshold

If Eve intercepts and resends, she introduces errors because she guesses Alice's basis 50% of the time. The QBER threshold for security is 11%. Above this, the key is insecure.

$$\text{QBER}_{\text{Eve}} = 25\%\text{ (intercept-resend)}$$

E91 Protocol (Ekert 1991)

Uses entangled photon pairs. Alice and Bob each measure their photon in one of 3 bases. CHSH inequality violation certifies security.

$$S = |E(a,b)-E(a,b')+E(a',b)+E(a',b')|\leq 2$$

Quantum correlations give $S=2\sqrt{2}$. Eve's presence reduces $S$ below $2\sqrt{2}$.

Decoy States

Practical QKD uses weak coherent pulses. A photon-number-splitting (PNS) attack targets multi-photon pulses. Decoy states (random intensity levels) detect PNS attacks.

Records & Status

  • Fiber QKD: 421 km (2023, twin-field)
  • Satellite: 7600 km (Micius, 2020)
  • Twin-Field QKD beats repeaterless bound $\propto\sqrt{\eta}$ vs $\eta$
  • One-time pad: information-theoretically secure if key $\geq$ message length

Bennett & Brassard, IEEE Int. Conf. (1984); Ekert, PRL 67, 661 (1991)

DLCZ Protocol (Duan–Lukin–Cirac–Zoller 2001)

Atomic ensembles are used as quantum memories. A write laser creates a single Stokes photon heralding storage of one collective excitation.

$$|\psi\rangle = |0\rangle + \sqrt{p_{\rm exc}}\,a^\dagger_{\rm exc}|{\rm vac}\rangle$$

Elementary Link

Two adjacent nodes each emit a herald photon. A 50/50 beamsplitter and coincidence detection creates a heralded Bell pair.

$$P_{\rm link} = \frac{p^2 \eta_d}{2}$$

This is probabilistic but heralded — we know when it succeeds without measuring the stored excitation.

Entanglement Swapping

Once two adjacent links are established, a Bell State Measurement on the inner node's two memories produces entanglement across the outer nodes.

$$\bar t_n = \frac{3}{2}(1+\frac{1}{2}+\cdots+\frac{1}{n})\,t_0$$

Three Generations of Repeaters

  • Gen 1: DLCZ — heralded entanglement, no error correction
  • Gen 2: Error-detected repeaters with quantum memories
  • Gen 3: Fault-tolerant (GKP encoding), all-optical

Current Status

Best demonstrations: $\leq$ 100 km elementary links. Multimode memories (AFC, gradient echo) allow simultaneous modes to boost rate.

Duan et al., Nature 414, 413 (2001); Briegel et al., PRL 81, 5932 (1998)

Quantum Error Correction

No-cloning prevents copying qubits, but errors can be detected and corrected without measuring the encoded information using ancilla qubits and syndrome measurements.

3-Qubit Bit-Flip Code

$$|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle+\beta|1\rangle \xrightarrow{\rm encode} \alpha|000\rangle+\beta|111\rangle$$

A single bit-flip error on any qubit is detected by comparing pairs. Syndrome bits $s_1=Z_1Z_2$, $s_2=Z_2Z_3$ identify which qubit flipped.

Surface Code

A 2D array of data qubits with alternating X and Z stabilizer checks. A $d\times d$ surface code encodes 1 logical qubit in $d^2$ physical qubits.

$$P_L \approx A\left(\frac{p}{p_{\rm th}}\right)^{\lceil d/2\rceil}$$

Threshold $p_{\rm th}\approx 1\%$. Below threshold, increasing $d$ exponentially suppresses logical errors.

Stabilizer Formalism

An $[[n,k,d]]$ code encodes $k$ logical qubits in $n$ physical qubits with distance $d$ (minimum weight of correctable errors).

Milestones

  • Google Sycamore: surface code below threshold (2023)
  • Magic state distillation for non-Clifford gates
  • Threshold theorem: fault-tolerant QC possible if $p < p_{\rm th}$
  • CSS codes: separate X and Z errors independently

Shor (1995); Steane (1996); Fowler et al., PRA 86, 032324 (2012)

Quantum Internet Stack

Analogous to the classical TCP/IP model, the quantum internet requires a layered architecture.

  • Physical: Photon transmission, waveguides, free-space optics
  • Link: Heralded entanglement generation between adjacent nodes
  • Network: Routing, entanglement swapping across multiple hops
  • Transport: QKD sessions, end-to-end entanglement
  • Application: Distributed quantum computing, blind QC, clock sync

Wehner–Elkouss–Hanson Roadmap

  • Stage 1: Trusted repeater network (classical relay)
  • Stage 2: Prepare-and-measure QKD
  • Stage 3: Entanglement distribution
  • Stage 4: QKD network (device-independent)
  • Stage 5: Quantum memory network
  • Stage 6: Quantum computing network

Quantum Routing

$$|\Phi^+\rangle_{AB}\otimes|\Phi^+\rangle_{BC}\xrightarrow{\rm BSM@B}|\Phi^+\rangle_{AC}$$

A quantum router performs Bell measurements to extend entanglement across the network without ever measuring the logical qubit content.

Blind Quantum Computing

A client with only a quantum channel can delegate computation to a server without the server learning the computation or result — verified by quantum mechanics.

Wehner, Elkouss, Hanson, Science 362, eaam9288 (2018)

Linear Optical Quantum Computing (KLM)

Knill, Laflamme & Milburn (2001): single photons + beamsplitters + phase shifters + photon-number-resolving detectors + ancilla photons = universal quantum computing.

$$P(\text{output}) = |\text{Perm}(U_{S,T})|^2$$

The probability of a given output configuration is the squared permanent of the relevant submatrix of the unitary $U$. Computing permanents is #P-hard classically.

Boson Sampling

$n$ indistinguishable photons injected into an $m$-mode random interferometer. Sampling the output distribution is believed classically hard for $n \gtrsim 50$.

$$|\psi_{\rm out}\rangle = U_{\rm Haar}|\psi_{\rm in}\rangle$$

Gaussian Boson Sampling (GBS)

Squeezed states replace single photons. Xanadu's Borealis (2022): 216 squeezed modes — demonstrates quantum advantage over classical simulation.

Fault-Tolerant Photonics

PsiQuantum uses photonic qubits with GKP (Gottesman–Kitaev–Preskill) encoding for error correction. Silicon photonics foundry manufacturing at scale.
