What is a Category?

A category $\mathbf{C}$ consists of:

  • A collection of objects $\mathrm{ob}(\mathbf{C})$
  • For each pair $A,B$: a set of morphisms $\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)$
  • An identity $\mathrm{id}_A: A\to A$ for each object
  • A composition law: if $f:A\to B$ and $g:B\to C$ then $g\circ f:A\to C$

Axioms

$$\mathrm{id}_B\circ f = f = f\circ\mathrm{id}_A$$
$$(h\circ g)\circ f = h\circ(g\circ f)$$

Commutative Diagrams

A diagram commutes when all paths between two objects compose to the same morphism. This is the key language of modern mathematics.

$$g\circ f = h\circ k \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \text{square commutes}$$

Penrose Connection

Category theory unifies disparate mathematical structures under one roof. Penrose emphasizes that the "sameness" of mathematical structures — isomorphism — is categorical at heart.

Functors

A functor $F:\mathbf{C}\to\mathbf{D}$ maps objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms, preserving structure:

$$F(\mathrm{id}_A) = \mathrm{id}_{F(A)}$$
$$F(g\circ f) = F(g)\circ F(f)$$

Functors are the "structure-preserving maps" between categories.

Natural Transformations

A natural transformation $\eta: F\Rightarrow G$ assigns to each object $A\in\mathbf{C}$ a morphism $\eta_A: F(A)\to G(A)$ such that for every $f:A\to B$:

$$\eta_B\circ F(f) = G(f)\circ\eta_A$$

This is the naturality square. Natural transformations are morphisms between functors.

Examples

$\pi_1$: fundamental group functor $\mathbf{Top}_*\to\mathbf{Grp}$

$H_n$: homology functor $\mathbf{Top}\to\mathbf{Ab}$

Forgetful: $U:\mathbf{Grp}\to\mathbf{Set}$ "forgets" the group structure

det: $\det: GL_n\to\mathbb{R}^*$ is a natural transformation between functors $\mathbf{Ring}\to\mathbf{Grp}$

Yoneda Lemma

The deepest theorem in category theory. For any functor $F:\mathbf{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ and object $A$:

$$\mathrm{Nat}(\mathrm{Hom}(A,-),\, F)\;\cong\; F(A)$$

Natural transformations from the representable functor $\mathrm{Hom}(A,-)$ to $F$ are in natural bijection with elements of $F(A)$.

Key insight: "An object is completely determined by how other objects map into it." The Yoneda embedding $A\mapsto\mathrm{Hom}(A,-)$ is fully faithful — it preserves and reflects all structure.

$$\mathbf{C}\hookrightarrow[\mathbf{C}^{op},\mathbf{Set}]$$

Yoneda in Physics

The Yoneda perspective underlies holography: a bulk geometry is determined by boundary correlators — exactly the statement that the object (bulk) is determined by morphisms into it (boundary maps). Also central to algebraic geometry: a scheme is determined by its functor of points.

The Big Picture

Categories, functors, and natural transformations form a 2-category. Saunders Mac Lane: "Categories were defined in order to define functors, functors in order to define natural transformations."

Universal Properties

A universal property characterizes an object by the morphisms into or out of it — up to unique isomorphism.

Product $A\times B$

$$\forall Z,\, \exists!\langle f,g\rangle: Z\to A\times B$$
$$\pi_1\circ\langle f,g\rangle = f,\quad \pi_2\circ\langle f,g\rangle = g$$

In $\mathbf{Set}$: Cartesian product. In $\mathbf{Top}$: product topology. In $\mathbf{Grp}$: direct product.

Coproduct $A+B$

The dual — arrows reversed. In $\mathbf{Set}$: disjoint union. In $\mathbf{Grp}$: free product. In $\mathbf{Top}$: disjoint union topology.

Pullback (Fiber Product)

$$A\times_C B = \{(a,b)\mid f(a)=g(b)\}$$

The "overlap" of $A$ and $B$ over $C$. Arises in algebraic geometry as fiber products of schemes.

Adjoint Functors $F\dashv G$

$$\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{D}}(FA,B)\cong\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{C}}(A,GB)$$

A natural bijection between hom-sets. Equivalently: there exist natural transformations called the unit and counit:

$$\eta: 1_{\mathbf{C}}\Rightarrow GF \quad\text{(unit)}$$
$$\varepsilon: FG\Rightarrow 1_{\mathbf{D}} \quad\text{(counit)}$$

satisfying the triangle identities: $(G\varepsilon)\circ(\eta G)=\mathrm{id}_G$ and $(\varepsilon F)\circ(F\eta)=\mathrm{id}_F$.

Examples

  • Free $\dashv$ Forgetful: $\mathrm{Free}:\mathbf{Set}\to\mathbf{Grp}$, $U:\mathbf{Grp}\to\mathbf{Set}$. Unit $\eta_S: S\to U(\mathrm{Free}(S))$ includes $S$ as generators.
  • Tensor $\otimes$ $\dashv$ internal hom $[{-},{-}]$: underlies currying in type theory
  • $\Sigma\dashv\pi$ in dependent types: existential $\dashv$ universal quantifier
  • Suspension $\dashv$ loop space in homotopy theory
  • Quantization $\dashv$ classical limit in physics

"Adjoint functors arise everywhere." — Saunders Mac Lane

What is a Topos?

A topos is a category that behaves like $\mathbf{Set}$: it has finite limits, exponentials, and a subobject classifier $\Omega$.

Subobject Classifier

$$\chi_S: X\to\Omega,\quad \chi_S(x)=\begin{cases}\top & x\in S\\ \bot & x\notin S\end{cases}$$

In $\mathbf{Set}$, $\Omega=\{\top,\bot\}$. A subobject $S\hookrightarrow X$ is classified by its characteristic function into $\Omega$.

Intuitionistic Logic

In a general topos, $\Omega$ is a Heyting algebra — it may have more than 2 truth values. Key: $\neg\neg p\neq p$ in general ("not not true" ≠ "true").

$$\Omega_{\mathrm{Sh}(X)} = \text{open sets of }X$$

Sheaves

For a topological space $X$, a sheaf $F$ assigns a set $F(U)$ (local sections) to each open $U\subseteq X$, with compatible restriction maps:

$$F:\mathrm{Open}(X)^{op}\to\mathbf{Set}$$

Sheaves on $X$ form a topos $\mathrm{Sh}(X)$.

Physics: Gauge Fields as Sheaves

A gauge field (connection on a principal bundle) is precisely a sheaf of local trivializations. The transition functions encode the non-triviality — and are exactly the cocycles in Čech cohomology.

$$A_\mu\;dx^\mu \;\longleftrightarrow\; \text{connection 1-form on }P\to M$$

Presheaf Toposes & Many Truth Values

For the category $\mathbf{C}$, the presheaf topos $[\mathbf{C}^{op},\mathbf{Set}]$ has a rich subobject classifier. A sieve on object $U$ is a collection of morphisms into $U$ closed under precomposition. Then:

$$\Omega(U)=\{\text{sieves on }U\}$$

This has many more than 2 truth values — modal and temporal logics naturally arise.

Forcing & Cohen Independence

Paul Cohen's 1963 proof that the Continuum Hypothesis ($\neg$CH) is independent of ZFC uses a forcing topos. The forcing poset $\mathbb{P}$ defines a Grothendieck topos $\mathrm{Sh}(\mathbb{P})$ in which:

  • Truth values = upward-closed sets in $\mathbb{P}$ (a Heyting algebra)
  • Excluded middle fails: $p\vee\neg p\neq\top$ for some conditions $p$
  • CH can be made false by adding generic reals

Topos theory gives a clean conceptual framework for set-theoretic forcing.

ZX-Calculus & Quantum Theory

Quantum mechanics can be formulated as a symmetric monoidal category with dagger structure $(\dagger)$. The ZX-calculus provides a graphical language for quantum circuits where:

  • Z-spiders (green) = Z-basis operations
  • X-spiders (red) = X-basis operations
  • Wires = qubit Hilbert spaces

Penrose: "The categorical viewpoint reveals the deep structural unity of physics and mathematics."

2-Categories

A 2-category has three levels:

  • 0-cells (objects): $A, B, C, \ldots$
  • 1-cells (morphisms): $f: A\to B$
  • 2-cells (morphisms between morphisms): $\alpha: f\Rightarrow g$

2-cells compose in two ways: vertically ($\alpha$ then $\beta$) and horizontally (using the interchange law).

$$(\beta\cdot\alpha)\circ(\delta\cdot\gamma)=(\beta\circ\delta)\cdot(\alpha\circ\gamma)$$

Atiyah-Segal TQFT Axioms

A topological quantum field theory in dimension $n$ is a symmetric monoidal functor:

$$Z:\mathbf{Cob}_n\to\mathbf{Vect}_\mathbb{C}$$

where $\mathbf{Cob}_n$ has closed $(n{-}1)$-manifolds as objects and $n$-dimensional cobordisms as morphisms.

  • A circle $S^1$ maps to a vector space $Z(S^1)=H$ (Hilbert space)
  • A pair-of-pants cobordism maps to a linear map $H\otimes H\to H$ (multiplication)
  • A cap (disk bounding $S^1$) maps to a trace $H\to\mathbb{C}$

Cobordism Hypothesis (Baez-Dolan)

Fully extended TQFTs $Z:\mathbf{Cob}_n^{fr}\to\mathcal{C}$ (with framings) are classified by fully dualizable objects in $\mathcal{C}$:

$$\{\text{fully ext. fr. TQFTs}\}\;\cong\;\{\text{fully dualizable obj. in }\mathcal{C}\}$$

Proved by Lurie (2009) using $(\infty,n)$-categories. This encodes the entire TQFT from the value on a point.

A-model & B-model

Witten's topological string theories are TQFTs on Riemann surfaces:

  • A-model: functor from $\mathbf{Cob}_2\to\mathbf{Fuk}(X)$ (Fukaya category of a symplectic manifold $X$). Counts holomorphic curves — gives Gromov-Witten invariants.
  • B-model: functor to $D^b\mathrm{Coh}(X^\vee)$ (derived category of coherent sheaves on the mirror $X^\vee$). Mirror symmetry is the equivalence $\mathbf{Fuk}(X)\simeq D^b\mathrm{Coh}(X^\vee)$ — Kontsevich's homological mirror symmetry conjecture.

$(\infty,n)$-Categories

An $(\infty,1)$-category has objects, morphisms, homotopies between morphisms, homotopies between homotopies, … with all higher morphisms invertible. These are modeled by quasi-categories (Joyal) or complete Segal spaces (Rezk). The $(\infty,1)$-category of spaces is the homotopy hypothesis: groupoids $\leftrightarrow$ homotopy types.

Lurie's "Higher Topos Theory" and "Higher Algebra" provide the technical foundation for modern derived algebraic geometry and factorization algebras in QFT.
