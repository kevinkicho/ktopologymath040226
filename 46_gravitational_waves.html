Gravitational Wave Generation

$$P = -\frac{G}{5c^5}\overset{...}{Q}_{ij}\overset{...}{Q}^{ij}$$

The quadrupole formula gives the power radiated in gravitational waves. $Q_{ij}$ is the mass quadrupole moment — only changing quadrupoles radiate.

Chirp Mass

$$\mathcal{M} = \frac{(m_1 m_2)^{3/5}}{(m_1+m_2)^{1/5}}$$

The chirp mass is the most precisely measured quantity from the gravitational waveform — it governs the rate of frequency increase ("chirp").

Inspiral Timescale

$$T_\text{merge} = \frac{12}{19}\frac{c_0^4}{\beta}\int_0^{e_0}\frac{e^{29/19}(1+\frac{121}{304}e^2)^{1181/2299}}{(1-e^2)^{3/2}}de$$

For circular orbits: $T \approx \frac{15}{304}\frac{a_0^4 c^5}{G^3 m_1 m_2 (m_1+m_2)}$

Orbital Decay (Peters)

$$\frac{da}{dt} = -\frac{64}{5}\frac{G^3 m_1 m_2(m_1+m_2)}{c^5 a^3}$$

The orbit shrinks as energy is radiated away. As $a\to 0$, frequency diverges — the "chirp." At merger, $f_\text{GW} \sim 2 f_\text{orb}$, reaching ~150 Hz for stellar-mass BHs.

Michelson Interferometer

A laser beam is split at a beam splitter. Light travels down two perpendicular 4-km arms, reflects off mirrors, and recombines. Without a GW, arms are equal → destructive interference → dark output.

$$\Delta\phi = \frac{4\pi L}{\lambda}h(t)$$

A passing GW stretches one arm by $+\frac{1}{2}hL$ and compresses the other by $-\frac{1}{2}hL$, creating a phase difference and a measurable light signal.

Fabry-Pérot Cavities

Each arm contains a Fabry-Pérot cavity (300 bounces): effective arm length ~1200 km, boosting sensitivity 300×.

Power Recycling

$$\text{Circulating power} \approx 100\text{ kW (from 200 W input)}$$

A recycling mirror reflects the output back in, building up laser power and reducing shot noise (quantum noise from photon counting statistics).

Noise Floor

Below $\sim$10 Hz: seismic noise. 10–100 Hz: thermal noise. Above 100 Hz: quantum (shot) noise dominates. LIGO is most sensitive at 100–300 Hz, $h\sim3\times10^{-24}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$.

Two Detectors

Hanford (WA) + Livingston (LA) are 3001 km apart — a GW signal arrives $\leq$10 ms apart, confirming real astrophysical origin vs. local noise. Three or more detectors triangulate sky position.

The Chirp Signal

GW150914 swept from ~35 Hz to ~150 Hz in 0.2 seconds — the characteristic "chirp" of a binary inspiral. Both LIGO detectors saw the signal with 7ms time offset.

Matched Filtering

$$\frac{S}{N} = \frac{\langle s\,|\,h\rangle}{\sqrt{\langle h\,|\,h\rangle}}$$

Where $\langle a|b\rangle = 4\,\text{Re}\int_0^\infty \frac{\tilde{a}^*(f)\tilde{b}(f)}{S_n(f)}\,df$ and $S_n(f)$ is the noise power spectral density. LIGO achieved $\text{SNR}\approx24$ combined.

Multimessenger Astronomy

GW170817 (Aug 17, 2017): first neutron star merger observed in both gravitational waves AND light — the dawn of multimessenger astronomy.

  • GW signal detected by LIGO+Virgo
  • GRB 170817A detected 1.7s later (Fermi+INTEGRAL)
  • Optical kilonova AT2017gfo in NGC 4993
  • X-ray/radio afterglow weeks later

Hubble Constant

$$H_0 = 70^{+12}_{-8}\text{ km s}^{-1}\text{Mpc}^{-1}$$

GW "standard sirens" give an independent measurement of $H_0$ — no distance ladder needed. Future detections will sharpen this dramatically.

Speed of Gravity

$$|v_\text{GW} - c|/c < 5\times10^{-16}$$

The 1.7s GW-GRB delay over $\sim40$ Mpc constrains GW speed to equal $c$ to 15 decimal places — ruling out many modified gravity theories.

Neutron Star EOS

Tidal deformability from GW170817 constrains the neutron star radius to $\sim 11$–13 km and rules out very stiff equations of state.

Quasi-Normal Modes

$$f_\text{QNM} \approx \frac{c^3}{2\pi G M}\left(1 - 0.63(1-\chi)^{0.3}\right)$$

Where $\chi = a/M$ is the dimensionless spin. Each QNM frequency is a unique fingerprint of the Kerr metric — testing the "no-hair" theorem.

Pulsar Timing Arrays

Arrays of ~70 millisecond pulsars (MSPs) act as a galactic-scale GW detector. GWs stretch/compress spacetime, causing correlated changes in pulse arrival times.

Hellings-Downs Correlation

$$\Gamma(\zeta)=\frac{3}{2}\frac{1-\cos\zeta}{2}\ln\!\left(\frac{1-\cos\zeta}{2}\right)-\frac{1}{4}\frac{1-\cos\zeta}{2}+\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\delta(\hat{\Omega})$$

Pairs of pulsars separated by angle $\zeta$ show a specific correlation pattern — the unique GWB signature (vs. clock noise which gives a flat correlation).

LISA Constellation

Three spacecraft in an equilateral triangle with 2.5 million km arms, trailing Earth by 20° in a heliocentric orbit. The triangle orientation rotates over 1 year.

Time-Delay Interferometry

$$X = (1-\hat{D}_{31}\hat{D}_{13})\left[y_{21}+\hat{D}_{12}y_{31}\right] - (1-\hat{D}_{12}\hat{D}_{21})\left[y_{31}+\hat{D}_{13}y_{21}\right]$$

TDI cancels laser frequency noise (dominant by $10^8$×) by combining time-delayed laser links. Essential because LISA arms are unequal in length and changing.

Source Classes

  • Massive BH mergers: $10^4$–$10^7\,M_\odot$ at $z\sim20$, SNR $\sim10^3$–$10^5$
  • EMRIs: compact object spiraling into MBH over $10^5$ cycles — extreme precision tests of GR
  • Verification binaries: AM CVn, HM Cnc (known WD-WD binaries already detected optically)
  • Stochastic: GWB from cosmological phase transitions

LISA Pathfinder

Launched 2015: demonstrated free-fall precision $5\times10^{-15}\,{\rm m}/\sqrt{\rm Hz}$, 100× better than required. LISA technology fully validated.
