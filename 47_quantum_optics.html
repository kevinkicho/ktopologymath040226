Optical molasses: six counter-propagating lasers tuned to $\delta = \omega_L - \omega_0 < 0$. Doppler shift brings laser into resonance for atoms moving toward the beam. Each photon absorption decelerates the atom by $\hbar k/m$.

Doppler Cooling

$F = -\alpha\, v,\quad \alpha = \hbar k^2 \frac{8|\delta|/\Gamma}{(1+4\delta^2/\Gamma^2)^2}$

A laser red-detuned by $\delta$ exerts a friction force on moving atoms. The Doppler-shifted frequency $\omega_L + kv$ is resonant when the atom moves toward the laser.

Doppler Limit

$T_D = \frac{\hbar\Gamma}{2k_B}$

For Rb-87 ($\Gamma/2\pi \approx 6\,\text{MHz}$), $T_D \approx 146\,\mu\text{K}$. This is the minimum temperature reachable by Doppler cooling alone.

Sub-Doppler Cooling

Sisyphus cooling (Cohen-Tannoudji): atoms climb potential hills created by polarization gradients, losing kinetic energy at each hill. Reaches $T \sim \mu\text{K}$, below $T_D$.

Magneto-Optical Trap

$F = -\alpha v - \kappa x$

A quadrupole magnetic field combined with polarized lasers provides both cooling and spatial confinement — a MOT. The workhorse of cold-atom labs worldwide.

1997 Nobel Prize

Steven Chu, Claude Cohen-Tannoudji, William D. Phillips — "for development of methods to cool and trap atoms with laser light."

Below $T_c$, bosons macroscopically occupy the ground state. Condensate fraction: $N_0/N = 1-(T/T_c)^3$. First achieved in 1995 with Rb-87 (Cornell & Wieman) and Na-23 (Ketterle).

Bose-Einstein Statistics

$\bar{n}_\epsilon = \frac{1}{e^{(\epsilon-\mu)/k_BT}-1}$

Bosons pile up in the lowest energy state when the chemical potential $\mu \to 0^-$.

Critical Temperature

$T_c = \frac{2\pi\hbar^2}{mk_B}\!\left(\frac{N}{\zeta(3/2)V}\right)^{\!2/3}$

$\zeta(3/2)\approx 2.612$. When the thermal de Broglie wavelength $\lambda_T \sim n^{-1/3}$, quantum effects dominate and BEC occurs.

Condensate Fraction

$\frac{N_0}{N} = 1 - \left(\frac{T}{T_c}\right)^3$

Macroscopic Wave Function

$i\hbar\,\partial_t\Psi = \left(-\frac{\hbar^2\nabla^2}{2m}+V+g|\Psi|^2\right)\Psi$

The Gross-Pitaevskii equation governs the condensate order parameter $\Psi(\mathbf{r},t)$. Superfluidity, vortices, and interference all follow from this single equation.

2001 Nobel Prize

Eric A. Cornell, Carl E. Wieman, Wolfgang Ketterle — "for the achievement of Bose-Einstein condensation in dilute gases of alkali atoms."

Gradient force: $F \propto \nabla|E|^2$ — points toward intensity maximum (beam focus). Scattering force pushes along beam axis. The gradient force can overcome scattering to form a 3-D trap.

Gradient Force

$\mathbf{F}_\text{grad} = \frac{\alpha}{2}\nabla|\mathbf{E}|^2 \propto \nabla I$

A dielectric particle (polarizability $\alpha > 0$) is attracted toward the highest intensity — the beam focus. This is the restoring force of the tweezer.

Trap Stiffness

$U(r) = -\frac{\alpha}{2}|E_0|^2 e^{-2r^2/w_0^2}$
$\kappa \approx \frac{4\alpha I_0}{w_0^2 c}$

Near the focus the trap is harmonic with stiffness $\kappa$. Brownian motion obeys $\langle x^2\rangle = k_BT/\kappa$ (equipartition).

Rayleigh vs Mie

Rayleigh regime: particle radius $a \ll \lambda$ — gradient force dominates. Mie regime: $a \sim \lambda$ — ray optics picture; refraction through particle redirects photon momentum.

Biology Applications

Single-molecule DNA stretching, kinesin motor force measurement, cell sorting, virus trapping. Forces measured in piconewtons — exactly the scale of molecular motors.

2018 Nobel Prize

Arthur Ashkin — "for the optical tweezers and their application to biological systems." (Shared with Mourou & Strickland for CPA.)

1000× Peak Power: — 0.1x
CPA: stretch → amplify → compress. Time-bandwidth product: $\Delta t \cdot \Delta\nu \geq \frac{1}{4\pi}$. Peak powers up to $10^{15}$ W achieved — opening ultrafast science, fusion research, and precision surgery.

Chirped Pulse Amplification

$\omega(t) = \omega_0 + \beta\,t \quad\text{(linear chirp)}$

Stretching a pulse in time (chirping) reduces its peak intensity by the stretch factor, allowing safe amplification in the gain medium without damaging it.

Time-Bandwidth Product

$\Delta t \cdot \Delta\nu \geq \frac{1}{4\pi}$

A transform-limited pulse saturates this bound. After stretching by factor $M$ and recompressing: $\Delta t_\text{final} = \Delta t_\text{original}$ but amplitude is $M$ times larger, giving $M^2$ times more peak power.

Gain Media

Nd:glass, Ti:sapphire (800 nm, bandwidth ~100 nm). Ti:sapphire supports sub-10 fs pulses. Multi-pass and regenerative amplifiers boost energy from nJ to J.

Applications

  • LASIK eye surgery
  • Nuclear fusion ignition (NIF)
  • Laser-driven particle acceleration
  • Ultrafast spectroscopy (femtochemistry)
  • ELI-NP: $10^{23}$ W/cm²

2018 Nobel Prize

Gérard Mourou & Donna Strickland — "for their method of generating high-intensity, ultra-short optical pulses." (CPA, 1985.)

Phase space (quadrature plane $X_1$–$X_2$). Coherent state: circular uncertainty disk (vacuum noise $= \tfrac{1}{2}$). Squeezed state: ellipse — noise reduced in one quadrature at the cost of the conjugate. $\Delta X_1 \cdot \Delta X_2 \geq \tfrac{1}{4}$ (Heisenberg).

Coherent States

$|\alpha\rangle = D(\alpha)|0\rangle,\quad \hat{a}|\alpha\rangle = \alpha|\alpha\rangle$

Eigenstate of the annihilation operator $\hat{a}$. Minimum-uncertainty state with equal noise in both quadratures — closest quantum analogue to a classical field.

Squeezed States

$S(r,\varphi)|\alpha\rangle,\quad S = e^{\frac{r}{2}(e^{-2i\varphi}\hat{a}^2 - e^{2i\varphi}\hat{a}^{\dagger 2})}$
$\Delta X_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}e^{-r},\quad \Delta X_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}e^{r}$

Squeezing parameter $r$ redistributes quantum noise: reduced by $e^{-r}$ in the squeezed quadrature, amplified by $e^{r}$ in the anti-squeezed quadrature.

Heisenberg Uncertainty

$\Delta X_1 \cdot \Delta X_2 \geq \tfrac{1}{4}$

Squeezed states saturate this bound — they are minimum-uncertainty states. The product of noise in both quadratures equals exactly $\tfrac{1}{4}$.

Two-Mode Squeezing

$S_2(\zeta) = e^{\zeta^* \hat{a}\hat{b} - \zeta\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{b}^\dagger}$

Generates EPR-entangled pairs — the continuous-variable analogue of Bell states. Used in quantum teleportation of optical fields and CV quantum key distribution.

LIGO & Gravitational Waves

Advanced LIGO injects 15 dB of squeezed light into the dark port of the interferometer, reducing quantum shot noise by a factor of $\sim 5.6$ and extending the detection range by $\sim 40\%$. Demonstrated at design sensitivity since 2019.

Applications

  • Gravitational-wave detection (LIGO, Virgo)
  • Sub-shot-noise optical sensing
  • Continuous-variable QKD
  • Optical quantum computing (GKP encoding)
  • Quantum imaging beyond Rayleigh limit

Wigner function $W(x,p)$ — quantum phase-space quasi-probability distribution. Negativity (blue regions) is a signature of non-classicality absent in any classical probability distribution.

Wigner Function

$W(x,p) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\langle x-y|\hat{\rho}|x+y\rangle e^{2ipy}\,dy$

A quasi-probability distribution on phase space. Unlike a classical probability distribution, $W$ can be negative — a hallmark of genuine quantum behaviour.

Fock States $|n\rangle$

$W_n(x,p) = \frac{(-1)^n}{\pi}e^{-2(x^2+p^2)}L_n\!\left(4(x^2+p^2)\right)$

$L_n$ are Laguerre polynomials. The function has $n$ oscillating annular lobes with alternating sign — $W_n$ is negative at the origin for odd $n$, and shows $n$ radial zero-crossings.

Non-Classicality

Any state with $W(x,p) < 0$ somewhere has no classical analogue. Coherent states and thermal states have non-negative $W$; all Fock states $|n\geq 1\rangle$ and Schrödinger cats are non-classical.

Schrödinger Cat State

$|\text{cat}\rangle = \frac{|\alpha\rangle + |-\alpha\rangle}{\sqrt{2(1+e^{-2|\alpha|^2})}}$

Exhibits interference fringes between the two coherent-state blobs — the fringes are highly negative and are the classical field equivalent of quantum interference.

Quantum Tomography

$W(x,p)$ is reconstructed experimentally via homodyne detection — measuring rotated quadratures $X_\theta = x\cos\theta + p\sin\theta$ and applying inverse Radon transform. Achieves full quantum state tomography of optical pulses.

Related Distributions

  • Husimi $Q$-function: smoothed $W$, always $\geq 0$
  • Glauber–Sudarshan $P$-function: diagonal in coherent-state basis; singular for non-classical states
  • $W$, $Q$, $P$ form an $s$-ordered family parameterised by $s \in [-1,+1]$

Jaynes–Cummings model: two-level atom coupled to a single cavity mode. Strong coupling $g \gg \kappa, \gamma$ — energy exchange faster than decay. Vacuum Rabi splitting $2g$ visible in transmission spectrum.

Jaynes–Cummings Hamiltonian

$H = \hbar\omega\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} + \frac{\hbar\omega_0}{2}\hat{\sigma}_z + \hbar g(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{\sigma}^- + \hat{a}\hat{\sigma}^+)$

$g$ = vacuum Rabi coupling, $\kappa$ = cavity decay rate, $\gamma$ = spontaneous emission rate. Exactly solvable — the cornerstone of quantum optics theory.

Dressed States

$|\pm,n\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(|e,n\rangle \pm |g,n+1\rangle\right)$
$E_{\pm,n} = \hbar\left(n+\tfrac{1}{2}\right)\omega \pm \hbar g\sqrt{n+1}$

The Jaynes–Cummings ladder is anharmonic — level spacing scales as $\sqrt{n}$. This anharmonicity underlies photon blockade.

Strong Coupling Regime

$g \gg \kappa,\, \gamma$

Coherent atom–photon exchange dominates over all decay channels. First achieved by Kimble (1992) and Rempe with Rydberg atoms. Enables deterministic quantum gates.

Photon Blockade

$\frac{g^2}{\kappa} \gg 1 \implies g^{(2)}(0) \ll 1$

A single photon shifts the cavity resonance by $\sim g^2/\Delta$, blocking entry of a second photon. Creates a deterministic single-photon source with $g^{(2)}(0) \to 0$.

Circuit QED

Superconducting transmon qubits coupled to microwave coplanar waveguide resonators achieve $g/2\pi \sim 100\,\text{MHz}$, $g/\kappa \sim 10^4$. The dominant architecture for quantum computing (Google, IBM, Rigetti).

Nobel Prize 2012

Serge Haroche — "for ground-breaking experimental methods that enable measuring and manipulation of individual quantum systems." Haroche counted single photons in a microwave cavity without destroying them using circular Rydberg atoms.

SPDC: pump → signal + idler (energy & momentum conserved). HOM effect: two identical photons on a 50/50 beamsplitter always exit the same port — quantum interference of bosons.

Spontaneous Parametric Down-Conversion

$\hbar\omega_p = \hbar\omega_s + \hbar\omega_i,\quad \mathbf{k}_p = \mathbf{k}_s + \mathbf{k}_i$

A $\chi^{(2)}$ nonlinear crystal converts one pump photon into a signal–idler pair. Phase matching (momentum conservation) restricts emission to specific angles (Type-I/II BBO, KTP, ppLN).

Photon Statistics: $g^{(2)}(\tau)$

$g^{(2)}(\tau) = \frac{\langle\hat{n}(t)\hat{n}(t+\tau)\rangle}{\langle\hat{n}\rangle^2}$
  • Coherent (laser): $g^{(2)}(0) = 1$ (Poissonian)
  • Thermal light: $g^{(2)}(0) = 2$ (bunching)
  • Single photon: $g^{(2)}(0) = 0$ (antibunching)

$g^{(2)}(0) < 1$ is a non-classical criterion — impossible for any classical electromagnetic field.

Hong-Ou-Mandel Effect

$|1,1\rangle \xrightarrow{\text{BS}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|2,0\rangle - |0,2\rangle)$

Two indistinguishable photons entering opposite ports of a 50/50 beamsplitter always bunch into the same output mode. HOM visibility $V = 1 - g^{(2)}_\text{HOM}(0)$ quantifies photon indistinguishability — critical for linear-optical quantum computing.

Single-Photon Emitters

  • NV centres in diamond: room temperature, spin-photon interface
  • SiV, GeV, SnV in diamond: near-transform-limited linewidths
  • InGaAs quantum dots: $g^{(2)}(0) < 0.01$, $V > 99\%$
  • hBN defects: 2D material, visible range

Boson Sampling

Photonic quantum advantage: $n$ photons through an $m$-mode interferometer sample a distribution that is classically $\#P$-hard to compute. Demonstrated beyond classical simulation by USTC (2020, Jiuzhang, 50+ photons).

Direct transmission: key rate $\propto e^{-\alpha L}$ (exponential decay, 0.2 dB/km at 1550 nm). Quantum repeater: polynomial scaling via entanglement swapping at intermediate nodes. Enables global quantum networks.

The Distance Problem

$R_\text{direct} \propto \eta_\text{ch} = e^{-\alpha L},\quad \alpha = 0.046\,\text{km}^{-1}$

Fiber loss (0.2 dB/km at 1550 nm) limits direct quantum communication to $\sim 300\,\text{km}$ for practical key rates. Classical amplifiers cannot clone quantum states (no-cloning theorem).

DLCZ Protocol

$|\Psi^-\rangle_{AB} \xrightarrow{\text{BSM}_C} |\Psi^-\rangle_{AC}$

Duan–Lukin–Cirac–Zoller (2001): atomic ensembles as quantum memories, entanglement generated by photon-mediated Bell measurements, swapping extends range step by step.

Entanglement Swapping

If nodes A–B and B–C share entanglement, a Bell-state measurement (BSM) at B teleports entanglement to A–C. The middle node is consumed but no quantum information travels the full distance as a single photon.

Quantum Memory

$\eta_\text{mem} = e^{-t_\text{wait}/T_2}$

Spin-photon interfaces (atomic ensembles, rare-earth crystals, NV centres) store entanglement while waiting for heralding signals. Coherence times $T_2 \sim$ seconds demonstrated (europium:YSO).

Satellite QKD

Micius satellite (USTC, 2016–): free-space QKD over 2,400 km Alice–Bob ground stations via satellite relay. Not a true quantum repeater but demonstrates intercontinental quantum communication. Entanglement distributed over 1,200 km (2017).

Quantum Internet Layers

  • Physical: photon generation, detection, fiber/free-space
  • Entanglement: Bell-state measurement, heralding, memories
  • Network: routing, entanglement purification
  • Application: QKD, blind quantum computing, distributed sensing

Photonic Quantum Computing

PsiQuantum (fusion-based), Xanadu (GBS), QuiX: million-qubit photonic chips projected using silicon photonics. Advantage: room-temperature operation, telecom wavelengths, standard fab.
