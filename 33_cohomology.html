de Rham's Theorem

H^k_dR(M) ≅ H^k(M; ℝ)

Betti numbers bₖ = dim Hᵏ count k-dimensional holes:

b₀ = connected components
b₁ = independent loops (handles)
b₂ = independent 2-cavities
χ = b₀ − b₁ + b₂ (Euler char.)
Gauss-Bonnet: ∫_M K dA = 2πχ(M) — curvature integrates to topology!
Hodge theorem: every cohomology class has a unique harmonic representative.

The Euler characteristic χ is a topological invariant — it doesn't change under continuous deformation.

Chern Classes

For a complex vector bundle E over M:

c₁(E) = [Tr F/2πi] ∈ H²(M,ℤ)
c₂(E) = [(TrF)²−(TrF²)]/8π²
det(1+itF/2π) = Σ cₖ(E) tᵏ

Chern-Weil theorem: invariant polynomials of curvature give integral cohomology classes.

de Rham Complex

Ω⁰ →^d Ω¹ →^d Ω² →^d Ω³
d² = 0 (d∘d = 0)
H^k_dR = ker(d:Ωᵏ→Ωᵏ⁺¹) / im(d:Ωᵏ⁻¹→Ωᵏ)

Closed forms: dω=0 (kernel). Exact forms: ω=dη (image). Cohomology = closed mod exact.

S¹: H⁰=ℝ, H¹=ℝ. The generator of H¹ is dθ — closed (d(dθ)=0) but not exact (θ is multivalued).
T²: H⁰=ℝ, H¹=ℝ², H²=ℝ. Two independent 1-forms: dθ₁ and dθ₂.
S²: H⁰=ℝ, H¹=0, H²=ℝ. No closed non-exact 1-forms — simply connected!

Key Principle

d² = 0 ⟹ im ⊂ ker

This is why cohomology is well-defined: every exact form is automatically closed.

de Rham's theorem: H^k_dR(M) ≅ H^k_singular(M; ℝ) — smooth and topological cohomology agree!

Čech Cohomology

Given a good cover {Uᵢ} of X, the Čech complex uses intersections:

Č⁰ = ∏ Uᵢ   Č¹ = ∏ Uᵢ∩Uⱼ   Č² = ∏ Uᵢ∩Uⱼ∩Uₖ
Ȟᵏ(X, ℤ) = Hᵏ(Č•)
Good cover: all intersections are contractible. Then Čech cohomology equals singular cohomology (Leray's theorem).

Nerve of a Cover

The nerve N(U) has one vertex per Uᵢ, one edge per Uᵢ∩Uⱼ≠∅, one triangle per Uᵢ∩Uⱼ∩Uₖ≠∅, etc.

S¹ with 3 sets: nerve = triangle boundary. H⁰=ℤ (connected), H¹=ℤ (one 1-cycle).
Add 4th set making triple overlap nonempty: nerve gains a filled triangle → becomes contractible → H¹=0!

Čech–de Rham Isomorphism

Ȟᵏ(M, ℝ) ≅ H^k_dR(M)

On smooth manifolds, Čech and de Rham cohomologies agree — two complementary views of the same topology.

Poincaré Duality

For a closed orientable n-manifold M:

Hᵏ(M) ≅ Hⁿ⁻ᵏ(M)
⟨[α],[β]⟩ = ∫_M α∧β

The wedge product and integration pair k-forms with (n-k)-forms non-degenerately.

Torus T² (n=2): H¹(T²)=ℝ². The pairing H¹×H¹→ℝ via ∫dθ₁∧dθ₂ = 1 is the intersection form.
Geometric picture: a 1-cycle γ and 1-cocycle ω — the integral ∫_γ ω counts how many times γ crosses the Poincaré dual of ω.

Poincaré Dual

PD([N]) = [ω_N] ∈ Hⁿ⁻ᵏ(M)

A k-dimensional submanifold N ↔ an (n−k)-cohomology class: ∫_N α = ∫_M α∧ω_N.

For the torus: the horizontal circle gives a class in H₁, its Poincaré dual is a 1-form whose integral across any transverse curve = intersection number.

Characteristic Classes

For a complex line bundle L (U(1) bundle) over M:

c₁(L) = [F/2π] ∈ H²(M, ℤ)

The first Chern class is the integral cohomology class of the curvature 2-form divided by 2π.

Integer obstruction: c₁ ∈ ℤ measures how twisted the bundle is. c₁=0 iff the bundle is trivial (globally).

Classifying Space

BU(1) = ℂP^∞
[M, ℂP^∞] ≅ H²(M, ℤ)

Any U(1) bundle over M is classified by a map M→ℂP^∞. The generator of H²(ℂP^∞)=ℤ pulls back to c₁.

ℂP¹ = S²: the Hopf fibration S³→S² is the non-trivial U(1) bundle with c₁=1.

Euler Class

e(E) ∈ Hⁿ(M) for rank-n bundle

The Euler class of the tangent bundle equals the Euler characteristic: ∫_M e(TM) = χ(M).

Gauss-Bonnet: ∫_M K dA = 2πχ(M). This is Euler class = Chern-Weil representative of e(TM).
Physical: c₁ counts vortex lines (Abrikosov vortices in superconductors) or magnetic monopoles. Each vortex contributes one flux quantum Φ₀ = h/e.

Sheaf Cohomology

A sheaf ℱ on X assigns groups ℱ(U) to open sets, with restriction maps. The cohomology Hᵖ(X, ℱ) measures global obstructions to patching local sections.

0 → H⁰(X,ℱ) → ∏ ℱ(Uᵢ) → ∏ ℱ(Uᵢ∩Uⱼ) → …
H⁰ = global sections (functions that agree everywhere). Higher Hᵖ = obstructions to gluing.

GAGA Principle

Hᵖ(X^an, ℱ^an) ≅ Hᵖ(X, ℱ)

Serre (1956): for a projective variety X over ℂ, algebraic and analytic cohomologies agree. Holomorphic = algebraic on compact spaces!

GAGA = Géométrie Algébrique et Géométrie Analytique. Bridging abstract algebra and complex analysis.

Penrose Transform

massless field of helicity s ↔ H¹(PT, O(−2s−2))

Twistor space PT = ℂP³\ℂP¹. Massless free fields on Minkowski space ↔ sheaf cohomology classes on twistor space.

s=1 (Maxwell): electromagnetic field ↔ H¹(PT, O(−4)). s=2 (gravity): graviton ↔ H¹(PT, O(−6)).
The Penrose transform converts PDE solutions into sheaf cohomology — topology encodes physics!
On ℂP¹: Hᵖ(ℂP¹, O(n)) = 0 for p=1, n≥−1. Riemann-Roch: χ(O(n)) = n+1 (for n≥0).
