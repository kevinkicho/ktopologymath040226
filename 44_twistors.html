Twistor Space

Twistor space $\mathbb{T}\cong\mathbb{C}^4$ is a complex 4-dimensional space. A twistor is a pair

$$Z^\alpha = (\omega^A,\,\pi_{A'})$$

where $\omega^A$ is a two-component complex spinor and $\pi_{A'}$ its dual. Projective twistor space is

$$\mathbb{PT}\cong\mathbb{CP}^3$$

Incidence Relation

A twistor $Z^\alpha$ is incident with a spacetime point $x^{AA'}$ when

$$\omega^A = i\,x^{AA'}\pi_{A'}$$

For fixed $x$, the set of incident twistors forms a CP¹ line (a Riemann sphere) inside $\mathbb{CP}^3$.

Duality

  • Point in Minkowski space → line in $\mathbb{PT}$
  • Twistor $Z^\alpha$ → null ray in Minkowski space
  • Two points are null-separated ↔ their CP¹ lines intersect
$$x^{AA'} \text{ null-sep from } y^{AA'} \iff L_x \cap L_y \neq \emptyset$$

This geometry elegantly encodes the causal structure of spacetime without coordinates.

Penrose Transform

Massless free fields on Minkowski space correspond to sheaf cohomology classes on twistor space:

$$\phi_{A_1\cdots A_{2s}}(x) = \oint_{\mathbb{CP}^1} \pi_{A_1}\cdots\pi_{A_{2s}}\,f(Z)\,d\pi$$

where $f\in H^1(\mathbb{PT}^+,\mathcal{O}(2s-2))$.

Double Fibration

$$\mathbb{M} \xleftarrow{q} \mathbb{F} \xrightarrow{p} \mathbb{PT}$$

$\mathbb{F}=\mathbb{M}\times\mathbb{CP}^1$ is the correspondence space. Pulling back cohomology from $\mathbb{PT}$ and integrating over the CP¹ fibers gives fields on $\mathbb{M}$.

Physics Dictionary

  • $s=\pm 3/2$: gravitino / graviton
  • $s=\pm 1$: photon (Maxwell)
  • $s=\pm\tfrac{1}{2}$: electron / Weyl neutrino
  • $s=0$: massless scalar (Higgs)

The transform is an isomorphism for helicity $s\neq 0$; for $s=0$ one uses a different cohomology group.

Hopf Fibration

The Hopf map $h: S^3\to S^2$ sends a unit quaternion to a point on the 2-sphere:

$$h(z_1,z_2) = \bigl(2\operatorname{Re}(z_1\bar{z}_2),\,2\operatorname{Im}(z_1\bar{z}_2),\,|z_1|^2-|z_2|^2\bigr)$$

Each fiber $h^{-1}(p)\cong S^1$ is a great circle in $S^3$. Projected stereographically to $\mathbb{R}^3$, these circles are Villarceau circles — every pair is linked exactly once.

Robinson Congruence

A single (non-null) twistor $Z^\alpha$ determines a Robinson congruence: a twisted family of null rays in Minkowski space whose cross-sections form circles sweeping out a torus — precisely the Hopf fibration.

$$Z^\alpha \longleftrightarrow \text{Robinson congruence (null geodesics)}$$

Spinor Connection

The Hopf fibration underlies spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ representations: a $2\pi$ rotation maps a spinor to its negative, requiring a full $4\pi$ to return. The fiber $S^1\subset S^3$ double-covers $SO(2)$.

$$\pi_3(S^2)\cong\mathbb{Z},\quad \text{generator = Hopf map}$$

There is also a quaternionic version: $S^7\to S^4$ with fibers $S^3$, related to octonions and the instanton moduli space.

Momentum Twistors

Hodges (2009) introduced momentum twistors $Z_i=(\lambda_i,\mu_i)$ where

$$\mu_i^{A'} = x_i^{AA'}\lambda_{iA}$$

Supermomentum conservation is automatic; the BCFW recursion becomes a simple geometric statement in $\mathbb{CP}^3$.

Amplituhedron

Arkani-Hamed & Trnka (2013): all tree-level and loop amplitudes of $\mathcal{N}=4$ SYM are encoded as the volume of a geometric object

$$\mathcal{A}_{n,k,L} \subset \text{Gr}(k,n)$$

called the amplituhedron, living in the Grassmannian. No Feynman diagrams needed — locality and unitarity emerge from the geometry.

BCFW Recursion

$$A_n = \sum_{\text{facets}} A_L \cdot \frac{1}{P^2} \cdot A_R$$

Each BCFW term triangulates the amplituhedron. The canonical form $\Omega$ has logarithmic singularities on all boundaries — the amplitude is its integral.

Volume Form

$$\Omega = \frac{\langle 1\,2\,3\,4\rangle^4}{\langle 1\,2\,3\,4\rangle\langle 2\,3\,4\,5\rangle\cdots\langle n\,1\,2\,3\rangle}$$

The angle brackets $\langle i\,j\,k\,l\rangle = \det(Z_i,Z_j,Z_k,Z_l)$ are the basic invariants of momentum-twistor geometry.

MHV Amplitudes

Maximally Helicity Violating (MHV) amplitudes are tree-level gluon scattering amplitudes with exactly two negative-helicity gluons. All others vanish (helicity conservation) or are MHV.

Parke-Taylor Formula (1986)

$$A_n(1^-\!2^-\!3^+\!\cdots n^+) = \frac{\langle 12\rangle^4}{\langle 12\rangle\langle 23\rangle\cdots\langle n1\rangle}$$

A single line replacing thousands of Feynman diagrams! The angle bracket is the spinor inner product $\langle ij\rangle = \epsilon_{AB}\lambda_i^A\lambda_j^B$.

Spinor-Helicity

Massless momenta are rank-1: $p^{AA'} = \lambda^A\tilde\lambda^{A'}$. Helicity $+1$ polarization uses $\tilde\lambda$; helicity $-1$ uses $\lambda$. The denominators $\langle i\,i{+}1\rangle$ encode collinear singularities.

$$[ij] = \epsilon_{A'B'}\tilde\lambda_i^{A'}\tilde\lambda_j^{B'}$$

Vanishing Amplitudes

  • All same helicity: $A_n(+\!+\!\cdots\!+) = 0$
  • One flip: $A_n(-\!+\!\cdots\!+) = 0$
  • Two flips: MHV — Parke-Taylor applies
  • Three flips: NMHV — needs BCFW recursion

The MHV amplitude is holomorphic in $\lambda_i$ — it only involves angle brackets, never square brackets. This holomorphicity is the seed of Witten's twistor string theory.

BCFW On-Shell Recursion

Britto, Cachazo, Feng, Witten (2005): shift two momenta by a complex parameter $z$:

$$\hat{p}_1(z) = p_1 + z\,\eta,\quad \hat{p}_2(z) = p_2 - z\,\eta$$

where $\eta^\mu$ is a reference null vector with $\eta\cdot p_1 = \eta\cdot p_2 = 0$.

Cauchy Residue Theorem

The shifted amplitude $A_n(z)$ is rational in $z$ and vanishes as $|z|\to\infty$. By Cauchy's theorem:

$$A_n(0) = -\sum_{\text{poles }z_i} \text{Res}_{z=z_i} A_n(z)$$

Each pole occurs when an internal propagator goes on-shell: $\hat{P}_I^2(z_i) = 0$.

Factorization

At each pole the amplitude factorizes into two lower-point on-shell amplitudes:

$$A_n = \sum_{I,h} A_L(\hat{1},\ldots,\hat{P}_I^{-h})\,\frac{i}{P_I^2}\,A_R(\hat{P}_I^h,\ldots,\hat{2})$$

Why It Works

The shift preserves momentum conservation and on-shell conditions for all external legs. Locality (poles only from propagators) and unitarity (factorization) are the only inputs — Feynman rules are not needed.

This recursion is what triangulates the amplituhedron: each BCFW term is a simplex in the geometric decomposition.
