The Mandelbrot Set — Formal Definition
$\mathcal M = \{c \in \mathbb{C} : \text{the sequence } (z_n) \text{ is bounded}\}$
where $z_0 = 0,\; z_{n+1} = z_n^2 + c$.

Main cardioid (period-1 bulb):
$c = \tfrac{\mu}{2} - \tfrac{\mu^2}{4},\; |\mu|<1$ — parametrised by $\mu \in \mathbb D$.
Approximate boundary: $|c - \tfrac{1}{4}| \approx \tfrac{1}{4}$ near $c=0$.

Period-2 bulb: centred near $c = -1$, where the 2-cycle $\{z_*, -z_* + c\}$ appears via pitchfork bifurcation.

Misiurewicz points: parameters where $z_0=0$ is pre-periodic (orbit eventually periodic but not periodic). Examples: $c=-2$ (fixed pre-period), $c = i$ (pre-period 2, period 2).

Logistic Map Connection
The logistic map $x_{n+1} = \lambda x_n(1-x_n)$ is conjugate to $z_{n+1}=z^2+c$ via the substitution:

$$c = \frac{\lambda}{2}\!\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{2}\right)$$
i.e.\ $c = \lambda/2 - \lambda^2/4$.

The real slice $c \in [-2, \tfrac{1}{4}]$ of $\mathcal{M}$ is in exact correspondence with $\lambda \in [1, 4]$ in the logistic map. Every bulb on the real axis maps to a stability window of the logistic map.
Shishikura's Theorem
Theorem (Shishikura, 1998):
$\dim_H(\partial \mathcal{M}) = 2$

The boundary of the Mandelbrot set has Hausdorff dimension exactly 2 — as large as possible for a curve-like object in $\mathbb{R}^2$. This is the same dimension as the plane itself, reflecting the infinite complexity of $\partial\mathcal M$.

Box-counting dimension: cover $\partial\mathcal M$ with boxes of side $\varepsilon$. Count $N(\varepsilon)$ boxes needed. Then:
$$\dim_H \approx \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0} \frac{\log N(\varepsilon)}{\log(1/\varepsilon)}$$ The slope of $\log N$ vs $\log(1/\varepsilon)$ approaches $2$.

Self-Similarity & Mini-Brots
The Mandelbrot set contains infinitely many mini-Mandelbrot sets (mini-brots) embedded in its own boundary — exact topological copies of $\mathcal M$ itself, at every scale. This is the hallmark of self-similarity.

Key locations:
• $c \approx -1.755$ — a mini-brot on the real axis
• $c \approx -0.1011 + 0.9563i$ — in the Douady rabbit region
• Every hyperbolic component's root has an associated mini-brot nearby

Böttcher Coordinates & External Rays
The Böttcher coordinate $\Phi: \mathbb{C}\setminus\mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{C}\setminus\overline{\mathbb{D}}$ is the conformal isomorphism that conjugates $f_c(z)=z^2+c$ (near $\infty$) to $w \mapsto w^2$:

$$\Phi(c) = \lim_{n\to\infty} [f_c^{\circ n}(c)]^{1/2^n}$$
External rays are the preimages of radial lines $\{re^{2\pi i\theta}: r>1\}$ under $\Phi$. The ray at angle $\theta$ is denoted $\mathcal{R}_\theta$.

Key landing points:
• $\theta = 1/2 \Rightarrow c = -2$ (tip of real axis)
• $\theta = 1/3, 2/3 \Rightarrow$ root of period-2 bulb ($c=-\tfrac{3}{4}$)
• $\theta = 1/7, 2/7, 4/7 \Rightarrow$ root of period-3 bulb
• Rational angles $p/q$ land at parabolic or Misiurewicz points.

Rational Rays & Bulb Roots
For a rational angle $\theta = p/q$ (in lowest terms), the external ray $\mathcal{R}_\theta$ lands at a parabolic point on $\partial\mathcal M$ that is the root of a period-$q$ bulb (or at a Misiurewicz point).

Pairs of rays land at the same point — they are the landing rays that separate the bulb from the rest of $\mathcal M$:

• Period-1 (main cardioid): rays $\{0\}$ (degenerate, tip $c=\tfrac{1}{4}$)
• Period-2 bulb root: rays $\tfrac{1}{3}$ and $\tfrac{2}{3}$ land at $c=-\tfrac{3}{4}$
• Period-3 bulbs: rays $\tfrac{1}{7}, \tfrac{2}{7}$ and $\tfrac{5}{7}, \tfrac{6}{7}$

The Douady–Hubbard theorem guarantees every rational ray lands.

Feigenbaum Universality
The Feigenbaum constants $\delta \approx 4.6692$ and $\alpha \approx 2.5029$ are universal: they appear in any unimodal map with a quadratic maximum — the logistic map, $\sin$-map, even physical systems.

Renormalization explanation: Define a doubling operator $T$ on unimodal maps by $Tf(x) = -\tfrac{1}{\alpha}f(f(-\alpha x))$. The Feigenbaum fixed point $f^* = Tf^*$ explains universality: all maps near the period-doubling limit converge to $f^*$ under $T$.

The eigenvalue of $DT$ at $f^*$ is $\delta$ — explaining why all period-doubling cascades share the same geometric ratio.
Renormalization Group
In statistical mechanics, the renormalization group (RG) describes how physical systems look at different length scales. The Mandelbrot set provides a mathematical model of RG ideas:

• The Feigenbaum doubling operator $T$ is an RG transformation in the space of unimodal maps.
• The fixed point $f^*$ is the RG fixed point — a critical system unchanged by coarse-graining.
• $\delta = 4.6692...$ is the relevant eigenvalue of the linearized RG, controlling the rate of approach to criticality.

This is identical in spirit to Wilson's RG for the Ising model, where the correlation length eigenvalue controls critical exponents.
Quantum Chaos
The boundary $\partial\mathcal M$ has Hausdorff dimension $\dim_H = 2$, matching the dimension of quantum chaotic wavefunctions in 2D:

• Quantum ergodicity (Shnirelman, Zelditch, Colin de Verdière): eigenfunctions of chaotic billiards equidistribute on the energy surface.
• The scarring phenomenon produces regions of anomalously high probability density — fractal-like structures with $\dim_H \approx 2$.

Lee–Yang zeros: the partition function of the 2D Ising model has zeros in the complex temperature/field plane distributed along curves that are Julia sets of associated rational maps — a direct connection between statistical mechanics and complex iteration.
Modular Groups & String Theory
The modular group $\text{SL}(2,\mathbb Z)$ acts on the upper half-plane $\mathbb H$ by Möbius transformations $\tau \mapsto \tfrac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d}$. This action shares deep structure with quadratic iteration:

• Both produce limit sets with fractal structure (the modular limit set is the real line; Julia sets are more intricate analogues).
• In string theory, the fundamental domain $\mathcal{F}$ of $\text{SL}(2,\mathbb Z)$ classifies inequivalent torus compactifications — the modular group is a duality symmetry.
• The Weierstrass $\wp$-function and $j$-invariant parametrise elliptic curves exactly as the Böttcher coordinate parametrises $\mathbb C \setminus \mathcal M$, suggesting a deep uniformisation principle connecting number theory, geometry, and iteration.
