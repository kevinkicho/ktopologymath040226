The Pentagrid Construction (de Bruijn 1981)
For each family $k \in \{0,1,2,3,4\}$, define parallel lines at angle $\theta_k = 2\pi k/5$:
$$G_k = \bigl\{\mathbf{r}\in\mathbb{R}^2 : \mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{e}_k = n + \gamma_k,\; n\in\mathbb{Z}\bigr\}$$ where $\mathbf{e}_k = (\cos\theta_k, \sin\theta_k)$ and $\gamma_k \in [0,1)$ is an offset parameter.

Key constraint: $\sum_{k=0}^{4}\gamma_k = 0$ ensures the grid avoids triple intersections (generic position).

Each intersection of families $j \neq k$ maps to a rhombus tile. The index vector $(m_0, m_1, m_2, m_3, m_4) \in \mathbb{Z}^5$ with $m_k = \lceil \mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{e}_k - \gamma_k \rceil$ projects two rhombus shapes: fat (72°) and thin (36°).
5D → 2D Projection
The tiling lives in a 2D physical space $E_\parallel$ embedded in $\mathbb{R}^5$. The projection vectors are:
$$\mathbf{v}^{(j)}_k = \cos(2\pi jk/5)\,\hat{e}_x + \sin(2\pi jk/5)\,\hat{e}_y \quad j=1,2$$ The perpendicular space $E_\perp \cong \mathbb{R}^3$ determines which lattice points project into the tiling window $W \subset E_\perp$. Changing $\gamma_k$ shifts the window, producing different (but locally indistinguishable) tilings.

Golden ratio in spacing: consecutive line spacings in family $k$ follow the ratio $\tau = \varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ — the same ratio as kite-to-dart tile counts.
Uncountably many tilings: Different $\gamma_k$ values give tilings that differ globally but are locally indistinguishable — any finite patch in one appears somewhere in every other. This is called local indistinguishability (Penrose 1979).

Quasicrystal Diffraction Theory
The structure factor for a set of atomic positions $\{\mathbf{r}_j\}$ is:
$$F(\mathbf{G}) = \sum_j f_j\, e^{i\mathbf{G}\cdot\mathbf{r}_j}$$ For a Penrose quasicrystal, reciprocal lattice vectors require 5 integer indices:
$$\mathbf{G} = \sum_{k=0}^{4} h_k \mathbf{G}_k, \quad \mathbf{G}_k = \frac{2\pi}{a}\bigl(\cos(2\pi k/5),\,\sin(2\pi k/5)\bigr)$$ This gives a dense (not periodic) but discrete set of Bragg peaks — the hallmark of quasicrystals: sharp diffraction spots with icosahedral/pentagonal symmetry.
Physical Quasicrystals
● Al−Mn (Shechtman 1982) — first icosahedral quasicrystal
● Al−Cu−Fe — thermodynamically stable icosahedral phase
● Ho−Mg−Zn — perfect dodecahedral macroscopic shape
● Icosahedrite (Bindi 2009) — natural quasicrystal in Khatyrka meteorite

IUCr redefined “crystal” in 1992: any solid with an essentially discrete diffraction diagram — removing the periodicity requirement entirely.
Crystallographic restriction theorem: A periodic tiling in 2D can only have 2-, 3-, 4-, or 6-fold rotational symmetry. 5-fold is forbidden for periodic structures — which is precisely why Penrose tilings and quasicrystals are so remarkable.

Substitution Matrix & Eigenvalue
The inflation rules $T\to G+T$, $G\to T+G$ are encoded in the matrix:
$$M = \begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&2\end{pmatrix}$$ Its eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda^2 - 3\lambda + 1 = 0$, giving
$$\lambda_+ = \varphi^2 = \varphi+1 \approx 2.618, \quad \lambda_- = \varphi^{-2} \approx 0.382$$ After $n$ steps: tile counts $\mathbf{v}_n = M^n\mathbf{v}_0 \sim \lambda_+^n\,\mathbf{u}_+$ where $\mathbf{u}_+\propto(\varphi,1)$.

Self-similarity: scaling the tiling by $\varphi^2$ and subdividing recovers the exact same tiling — a fractal-like structure at every scale.
Inflation vs. Deflation
Inflate: scale each tile by $\varphi$, then split according to substitution rules → more, smaller tiles covering the same area.

Deflate: merge groups of tiles into larger tiles → fewer, bigger tiles.

The two operations are exact inverses. Starting from any valid tiling, infinite inflation or deflation always produces another valid Penrose tiling. This proves that every finite patch appears infinitely often.
Why φ² = φ + 1 matters: the area of a fat rhombus to a thin rhombus is exactly φ². After each inflation step, the total tile count multiplies by π² ≈ 2.618 — not 2, not 3, but precisely the golden ratio squared.

Matching Rules & Aperiodicity
Penrose tiles carry edge markings (arrows or indentations) such that tiles may only be assembled if markings on shared edges agree. There are two types of edge markings:
● Single arrow (S): must align head-to-tail
● Double arrow (D): must also align head-to-tail

Theorem (Penrose 1974): Any tiling of the plane using P2 tiles that satisfies all matching rules is necessarily aperiodic — no translation symmetry is possible.

Undecidability connection: Berger (1966) proved that the domino tiling problem is undecidable. Penrose’s matching rules show that local constraints can enforce global aperiodicity — a deep connection to computability theory.
Conway Worms & Ammann Bars
Conway worms (John H. Conway): infinite sequences of kites and darts forming straight strips. Any Penrose tiling contains exactly 5 families of Conway worms, parallel to the 5 pentagrid directions.

Ammann bars (Robert Ammann): line segments on each tile that, when tiles obey matching rules, extend into 5 families of infinite parallel lines — with a quasiperiodic spacing ratio of $\varphi:1$. These bars directly encode the pentagrid structure.
Local vs. global: matching rules are purely local (only adjacent tiles need agree) yet they enforce a global structure. This is philosophically profound — Penrose himself connected this to the question of whether the laws of physics (local) could enforce the global structure of spacetime.

λ-Adic Numbers & Almost Periodicity

Harald Bohr (1925) defined almost-periodic functions: $f(x)$ such that for any $\varepsilon>0$, the set of $\tau$ with $\sup_x|f(x+\tau)-f(x)|<\varepsilon$ is relatively dense. Penrose tilings are almost-periodic in exactly this sense — they are the spatial analog of Bohr’s functions. The Fourier transform of an almost-periodic function is a discrete but dense set of frequencies, precisely matching quasicrystal diffraction.

Cut-and-Project & Model Sets

Yves Meyer (1972) introduced model sets (cut-and-project sets): take the integer lattice $\mathbb{Z}^5 \subset \mathbb{R}^5$, define a strip $\mathcal{S} = E_\parallel \times W$ where $W \subset E_\perp$ is a compact window (a regular pentagon for Penrose), and project $\mathcal{S} \cap \mathbb{Z}^5$ onto $E_\parallel$. The result is a Penrose tiling vertex set. Meyer’s theory proves these sets have pure point diffraction — the mathematical basis for sharp Bragg peaks in quasicrystals.

E₈ Root System & Icosahedral Quasicrystals

The $E_8$ root system has 240 vectors in $\mathbb{R}^8$. The icosahedral subgroup $Y \subset SO(3)$ of order 60 acts on a 6D sublattice of $E_8$. Projecting this onto 3D physical space plus 3D perpendicular space gives the 3D icosahedral quasicrystal. The same $E_8$ lattice appears in the heterotic string theory ($E_8 \times E_8$) and in Garrett Lisi’s $E_8$ theory of everything. The deepest tilings of physical space may be shadows of 8-dimensional geometry.

Kochen–Specker Contextuality

The Kochen–Specker theorem (1967) proves that quantum mechanics is contextual: measurement outcomes cannot be pre-assigned independently of which other observables are measured simultaneously. One construction uses a set of 31 rays in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with Penrose-like geometric structure. A Penrose tiling of the sphere gives a natural coloring problem whose unsolvability is the Kochen–Specker theorem — connecting aperiodic geometry directly to quantum foundations.

Noncommutative Geometry (Connes)

Alain Connes showed that the space of all Penrose tilings, while uncountable and topologically trivial (all tilings are locally indistinguishable), admits a rich noncommutative structure. The $C^*$-algebra $\mathcal{A}$ of the Penrose tiling has $K_0(\mathcal{A}) \cong \mathbb{Z}^2$ with the golden ratio $\varphi$ encoding the module structure. This is the first example of a physical application of noncommutative geometry — Connes used it as a motivating example in his 1994 book.

Penrose’s Quantum Mind Thesis

In The Emperor’s New Mind (1989) and Shadows of the Mind (1994), Penrose argues that human consciousness involves non-algorithmic processes, connected to his Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch-OR) hypothesis with Hameroff. The aperiodic structure of Penrose tilings mirrors his view: locally determined rules create globally non-computable structures. The undecidability of the tiling extension problem (can a finite patch extend to a full Penrose tiling?) connects directly to Gödel incompleteness.

“Road to Reality” connection: Penrose devotes Chapter 33 to quasicrystals and Chapter 1 to the golden ratio. The deeper point throughout the book is that the most profound mathematics — irrational numbers, infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces, fiber bundles — turns out to be exactly what describes physical reality. Penrose tilings are a perfect microcosm of this theme.
