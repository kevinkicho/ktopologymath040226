Vacuum Expectation Value

$\langle\phi\rangle = v/\sqrt{2} \approx 174\,\text{GeV}$

The Higgs field acquires a non-zero vacuum expectation value $v = 246\,\text{GeV}$ at the electroweak scale. This is the ground state of the universe after electroweak symmetry breaking.

Phase Transition

$V(\phi) = -\mu^2|\phi|^2 + \lambda|\phi|^4$

Above the electroweak temperature $T_{EW}\approx 159\,\text{GeV}$, the symmetry is restored and $v=0$ (symmetric phase). Below it, the field condenses into the broken phase with $v = 246\,\text{GeV}$.

Electroweak Scale

The value $v = 246\,\text{GeV}$ sets the scale of W and Z masses: $m_W = \frac{1}{2}gv \approx 80.4\,\text{GeV}$, $m_Z \approx 91.2\,\text{GeV}$.

Higgs Condensate

In the broken phase the Higgs field permeates all of space. Particles moving through it experience drag — their effective inertia is what we call mass. The Higgs boson is the quantum of fluctuation around $v$.

Mexican Hat Potential

$V(\phi)=-\mu^2|\phi|^2+\lambda|\phi|^4$

For $\mu^2>0$: the origin is a local maximum, and a circle of minima at $|\phi|=v/\sqrt{2}$ appears. The "bottom of the hat" is the degenerate vacuum manifold.

Goldstone Theorem

Orbiting the minimum ring costs no energy — these are massless Goldstone bosons. In the gauged electroweak theory, they are "eaten" by W and Z bosons, giving them longitudinal polarization and mass.

Radial Mode = Higgs

Oscillating radially (in and out from the minimum ring) costs energy proportional to $m_H^2 = 2\mu^2 = 2\lambda v^2$. This is the physical Higgs boson, discovered at 125 GeV.

$m_W=\tfrac{1}{2}gv\approx 80.4\,\text{GeV}$
$m_Z=\tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{g^2+g'^2}\,v\approx 91.2\,\text{GeV}$

The photon stays massless because the $U(1)_{EM}$ subgroup is unbroken.

Fermion Masses

$m_f = y_f \cdot v/\sqrt{2}$

Yukawa couplings $y_f$ range from $y_e\approx 2.9\times10^{-6}$ (electron) to $y_t\approx 1$ (top quark). Why these values differ by six orders of magnitude is the "flavor puzzle."

Higgs Mass

$m_H^2 = 2\lambda v^2 \approx (125\,\text{GeV})^2$

$\lambda\approx 0.13$ from $m_H=125\,\text{GeV}$. The hierarchy problem asks why $m_H^2$ is so small compared to the Planck scale $M_{Pl}^2$ given radiative corrections.

The Higgs mechanism in 3 acts: begin with a U(1) gauge theory + complex scalar; SSB generates a Goldstone mode θ that is unphysical; the gauge field "eats" θ becoming massive; only the radial fluctuation h (the Higgs boson) remains as a physical particle. Mass M = gv.

The Mechanism

$\phi = \dfrac{(v+h)\,e^{i\theta}}{\sqrt{2}}$

After SSB the field decomposes into: real radial mode $h$ (the Higgs boson) and angular mode $\theta$ (Goldstone). In unitary gauge $\theta \to 0$.

Gauge Transformation

$A_\mu \to A_\mu + \tfrac{1}{g}\partial_\mu\theta$

The Goldstone $\theta$ is pure gauge — it can be rotated away by a U(1) gauge transformation. This is the "eating."

Mass Acquisition

$\mathcal{L} \supset \tfrac{1}{2}g^2v^2 A_\mu A^\mu$

A mass term $M = gv$ appears for the gauge field. For the W boson: $g \approx 0.653$, $v = 246$ GeV → $M_W \approx 80$ GeV.

Physical DOF Count

Before SSB: massless gauge (2 DOF) + complex scalar (2 DOF) = 4 total.
After SSB: massive gauge (3 DOF) + real Higgs (1 DOF) = 4 total. Conservation of degrees of freedom.

Anderson-Higgs Mechanism

First described by Philip Anderson (1963) in superconductors, then applied to particle physics by Higgs, Brout-Englert, Guralnik-Hagen-Kibble (1964). The Nobel Prize went to Higgs and Englert in 2013.

The Higgs was discovered 4 July 2012 by ATLAS and CMS at the LHC. The two golden channels are H→γγ (diphoton bump at 125.09 GeV) and H→ZZ*→4ℓ (narrow 4-lepton peak). Both required >5σ significance. Combined signal strength $\mu = \sigma/\sigma_{SM} \approx 1.0$.

Discovery Channels

The Higgs was found via two rare but clean channels:

$H \to \gamma\gamma$   BR ≈ 0.23%
$H \to ZZ^* \to 4\ell$   BR ≈ 0.013%

Despite tiny branching ratios, these give sharp mass peaks over smooth backgrounds.

Production at LHC

$gg \to H$  (gluon fusion, dominant)
$qq' \to Hqq'$  (VBF)

Gluon fusion proceeds through a top quark loop — the top's large Yukawa coupling $y_t \approx 1$ makes it the dominant production mode.

5σ Discovery

Particle physicists require a signal-to-noise ratio of 5 standard deviations (p-value < 2.87×10⁻⁷) to claim discovery, corresponding to $\sim$1 in 3.5 million background fluctuations. The Higgs exceeded 5σ in both channels simultaneously.

Mass Measurement

$m_H = 125.09 \pm 0.24\,\text{GeV}$

The photon channel gives the most precise measurement due to excellent CMS/ATLAS EM calorimeter resolution $\sigma_m \approx 1.7$ GeV.

Feynman Diagrams

H→γγ: the Higgs couples to photons only through loops (W boson or top quark), since photons are massless. H→ZZ: tree-level through the HZZ vertex from $\mathcal{L} \supset \frac{m_Z^2}{v} h Z_\mu Z^\mu$.

After $\phi = (v+h)/\sqrt{2}$, the mass term $m_f = y_f v/\sqrt{2}$ and a Higgs-fermion coupling $-m_f h/v$ emerge simultaneously.

Fermion Mass Formula

$m_f = \dfrac{y_f\,v}{\sqrt{2}}, \quad y_f = \dfrac{m_f\sqrt{2}}{v}$

With $v = 246$ GeV. Yukawa couplings range from $y_e \sim 10^{-6}$ to $y_t \sim 1$.

The Hierarchy Problem

Why does $y_e / y_t \sim 3\times10^{-6}$? No SM explanation. Proposed solutions: Froggatt-Nielsen mechanism (flavor symmetries), extra dimensions (geography in the bulk), compositeness (partial compositeness). None confirmed yet.

Top Quark Special Role

$y_t \approx 1$  →  "natural" coupling

The top's near-unity Yukawa coupling makes it dominate Higgs production (gg→H via top loop), drive radiative corrections, and destabilize the vacuum (see Vacuum Stability tab).

Neutrino Masses

Neutrinos are massless in the SM ($y_\nu = 0$). Adding right-handed neutrinos with Yukawa coupling $y_\nu \lesssim 10^{-13}$ would give $m_\nu \lesssim 0.1$ eV. The seesaw mechanism offers an alternative: $m_\nu \sim y_\nu^2 v^2 / M_R$ with $M_R \sim 10^{14}$ GeV.

H-ff Coupling at LHC

$g_{Hff} = \dfrac{m_f}{v}$

Measured for t, b, τ, μ. All consistent with SM. Direct $H\to cc$ still below LHC sensitivity (2025).

Electroweak precision data constrain $S \approx 0.05\pm0.10$, $T \approx 0.09\pm0.12$. The $\rho$ parameter $\rho = M_W^2/(M_Z^2\cos^2\theta_W) \approx 1$ tests custodial symmetry.

Oblique Corrections

BSM physics that couples only to gauge bosons (not directly to fermions) affects precision observables through "oblique" (loop) corrections to gauge propagators.

$S = \dfrac{16\pi[\Pi_{33}(m_Z^2)-\Pi_{33}(0)]}{m_Z^2}$

$S$ parametrizes new states that mix with the $Z$; $T$ encodes isospin violation (custodial symmetry breaking); $U$ sensitive to mass splittings within new multiplets.

ρ Parameter

$\rho = \dfrac{M_W^2}{M_Z^2\cos^2\theta_W} \approx 1$

$\rho=1$ exactly in the SM at tree level, protected by custodial $SU(2)_C$ symmetry. Deviations signal new physics (e.g., an extra Higgs doublet, $\Delta\rho \sim g^2/(16\pi^2)$).

The SM Higgs quartic coupling $\lambda(\mu)$ runs to negative values at $\mu \approx 10^{10}$–$10^{11}$ GeV due to the large top Yukawa contribution. The measured point ($m_t = 173$ GeV, $m_H = 125$ GeV) sits in the metastable region: our vacuum is a false vacuum, stable on cosmological timescales (lifetime $\gg 10^{100}$ years) but not absolutely stable.

Effective Potential RGE

$\mu\dfrac{d\lambda}{d\mu} = \beta_\lambda$

The beta function $\beta_\lambda$ has a large negative contribution from the top Yukawa:

$\beta_\lambda \supset -\dfrac{3y_t^4}{2\pi^2}$

This drives $\lambda$ negative at high energies, destabilizing the potential.

Stability Conditions

Stable: $\lambda(\mu) > 0$ for all $\mu < M_{Pl}$
Metastable: $\lambda < 0$ but lifetime $\gg t_{universe}$
Unstable: $\lambda \ll 0$, fast tunneling

Current SM Status

With $m_t = 172.69$ GeV (PDG 2024) and $m_H = 125.09$ GeV, the SM sits at the edge of absolute stability — tantalizingly close to the metastability boundary. Some regard this as a hint of new physics at $\sim 10^{10}$ GeV.

Tunneling Rate

$\Gamma \sim e^{-S_E}$,  $S_E \sim \dfrac{2\pi^2}{3|\lambda|}$

The decay of our metastable vacuum proceeds by quantum tunneling, creating a bubble of true vacuum. The bubble wall expands at $c$. Estimated lifetime: $\tau \sim 10^{600}$ years — effectively eternal.

Implications

The near-criticality of our vacuum may be related to the anthropic principle, asymptotic safety of gravity, or BSM physics that stabilizes λ (e.g., supersymmetry). The stability boundary in ($m_t$, $m_H$) space is mapped by 3-loop RGE calculations.

Goldstone's theorem: if a continuous global symmetry $G$ is spontaneously broken to $H \subset G$, there is one massless Goldstone boson for each broken generator, i.e. $\dim(G) - \dim(H)$ Goldstone bosons. In a gauge theory, Goldstones are "eaten" (Higgs mechanism). Pions are pseudo-Goldstone bosons of approximate chiral symmetry $SU(2)_L \times SU(2)_R \to SU(2)_V$.

Goldstone's Theorem (1961)

$N_{GB} = \dim G - \dim H$

For every generator of $G$ not preserved by the vacuum, a massless spinless particle appears in the spectrum.

O(N) → O(N-1)

$N_{GB} = N-1$

E.g. $O(2) \to \{e\}$: 1 Goldstone (the angle θ). $O(3) \to O(2)$: 2 Goldstones (2-sphere angular directions). $O(4) \to O(3)$: 3 Goldstones — like Higgs SSB in the SM.

Pions as Pseudo-Goldstones

$SU(2)_L\times SU(2)_R \xrightarrow{\langle\bar{q}q\rangle} SU(2)_V$

Chiral symmetry is explicitly broken by small quark masses $m_u, m_d \neq 0$, so pions acquire a small mass via the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner relation:

$m_\pi^2 f_\pi^2 = (m_u+m_d)\langle\bar{q}q\rangle$

$m_\pi \approx 140$ MeV $\ll$ QCD scale $\Lambda_{QCD} \approx 200$ MeV — a pseudo-Goldstone boson signature.

Flat Direction

The Goldstone mode corresponds to motion along the "flat direction" of the potential — the degenerate vacuum manifold. It costs zero restoring force, hence zero mass.

Coleman-Mermin-Wagner

In 1+1 and 2+1 dimensions, quantum fluctuations can restore a continuous symmetry even at T=0, preventing SSB (and hence Goldstone bosons). Long-range order requires d ≥ 3+1 for relativistic systems.
