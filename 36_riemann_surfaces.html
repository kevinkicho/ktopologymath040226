Riemann Surface Basics

A Riemann surface resolves multi-valuedness by "stacking" copies of $\mathbb{C}$, one for each branch.

Branch formula: $z^{1/n} = r^{1/n} e^{i(\theta + 2\pi k)/n}$ for $k = 0, \ldots, n-1$

Each $k$ is one sheet. Moving through $\theta = 2\pi$ advances $k \to k+1 \pmod{n}$.

Monodromy: Looping once around $z=0$ permutes sheets cyclically: $\sigma = (0\;1\;\cdots\;n{-}1)$.

For $\log z$: $\log z = \ln r + i\theta$. Each $2\pi$ loop climbs to a new sheet — monodromy group $\cong \mathbb{Z}$, infinitely many sheets.

Branch cut: The thick dashed line on the negative real axis is where sheets connect. Crossing it moves you from sheet $k$ to sheet $k+1$.

Analytic Continuation

An analytic function defined on an open disk $D$ can be extended ("continued") to any overlapping disk where it converges, by matching values on the overlap.

Identity Theorem: If two analytic functions agree on a set with an accumulation point, they agree everywhere on the connected domain.

Monodromy Theorem: If a domain is simply connected (no holes), every analytic continuation returns to its starting value — no multi-valuedness.

Key example — $\sqrt{z}$:

  • Start at $z=1$, value $f=+1$.
  • Continue above real axis to $z=-1$: arrive at $f=+i$.
  • Continue below real axis to $z=-1$: arrive at $f=-i$.

Two paths, same endpoint, different values. Monodromy is non-trivial.

$\pi_1(\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$: loops around the puncture count how many branch sheets you traverse.

Riemann's insight: A multi-valued function IS single-valued on its Riemann surface. The surface is the natural domain.

Topological Classification

Every compact orientable surface is classified by its genus $g$ (number of handles).

Euler characteristic: $\chi = 2 - 2g$.

Examples as Riemann surfaces:

  • $g=0$ (sphere): $\mathbb{CP}^1 = \mathbb{C} \cup \{\infty\}$. All rational functions $f = P(z)/Q(z)$ live here.
  • $g=1$ (torus): $w^2 = z(z-1)(z-2)$ — three branch points give one handle.
  • $g=2$: $w^2 = z(z-1)(z-2)(z-3)(z-4)$ — five branch points.

Genus–degree formula: For $w^2 = P(z)$, $\deg P = 2g+1$ or $2g+2$ gives genus $g$.

Riemann–Hurwitz formula:

$\chi(S) = n\,\chi(\mathbb{CP}^1) - \sum_p (e_p - 1)$

where $n$ = degree of map, $e_p$ = ramification index at $p$.

Moduli space $\mathcal{M}_g$: The space of all complex structures on a genus-$g$ surface. $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} \mathcal{M}_g = 3g-3$ for $g \geq 2$.

$\mathcal{M}_1$ (elliptic curves): parameterized by $\tau \in \mathbb{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, $\dim = 1$.

Moduli Space & Teichmüller Theory

The moduli space $\mathcal{M}_g$ parametrizes all complex structures on a genus-$g$ surface up to biholomorphism.

$g=1$ case: every torus is $\mathbb{C}/\langle 1,\tau\rangle$ for $\tau\in\mathbb{H}$. Two tori $\tau,\tau'$ are isomorphic iff related by:

$$\tau' = \frac{a\tau+b}{c\tau+d},\quad \begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}\in\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$$

So $\mathcal{M}_1 = \mathbb{H}/\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, the yellow fundamental domain.

Fundamental domain: $\mathcal{F} = \{\tau : |\tau|\geq 1,\, |\mathrm{Re}(\tau)|\leq\tfrac{1}{2}\}$

Teichmüller space $\mathcal{T}_g$ is the universal cover of $\mathcal{M}_g$:

  • $\mathcal{T}_g$ is simply connected, contractible
  • $\dim_\mathbb{R}\mathcal{T}_g = 6g-6$ for $g\geq 2$
  • $\mathcal{M}_g = \mathcal{T}_g / \mathrm{MCG}_g$ where $\mathrm{MCG}_g$ is the mapping class group

Mapping class group $\mathrm{MCG}_g$ is generated by Dehn twists $T_\gamma$ along simple closed curves $\gamma$.

Weil-Petersson metric: $g_{WP}(\mu,\nu)=\int_\Sigma \mu\bar\nu\, dA/\|dz\|^2$ — a Kähler metric on $\mathcal{T}_g$.

String theory: the string partition function at genus $g$ is an integral over $\mathcal{M}_g$: $$Z_g = \int_{\mathcal{M}_g} \mathcal{F}_g(\tau,\bar\tau)\, d\mu_{WP}$$

UV finiteness of string theory follows from the compactness of $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g$ (Deligne-Mumford compactification).

Abel-Jacobi Map & Theta Functions

On a genus-$g$ surface $\Sigma$, choose a symplectic basis of cycles $(A_i, B_j)$ with $A_i\cap B_j=\delta_{ij}$.

Holomorphic 1-forms: $H^0(\Sigma,\Omega^1)$ is $g$-dimensional. Normalize basis $\omega_1,\ldots,\omega_g$ so $\oint_{A_i}\omega_j=\delta_{ij}$. Period matrix: $\Omega_{ij}=\oint_{B_i}\omega_j$.

Abel-Jacobi map: $$\text{AJ}: \Sigma\to J(\Sigma)=\mathbb{C}^g/(\mathbb{Z}^g+\Omega\mathbb{Z}^g)$$ $$p\mapsto\left(\int_{p_0}^p\omega_1,\ldots,\int_{p_0}^p\omega_g\right)$$

Jacobian variety $J(\Sigma)=\mathbb{C}^g/\Lambda$ is a $g$-dimensional complex torus (abelian variety).

Torelli theorem: $\Sigma$ is uniquely determined by its Jacobian $J(\Sigma)$ (for $g\geq 2$).

Theta function: $$\theta(z|\tau)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{i\pi n^2\tau + 2\pi i n z}$$

Quasi-periodicity: $\theta(z+1|\tau)=\theta(z|\tau)$, $\theta(z+\tau|\tau)=e^{-i\pi\tau-2\pi iz}\theta(z|\tau)$.

Riemann-Roch theorem: $$\ell(D)-\ell(K-D)=\deg(D)+1-g$$

where $K$ is the canonical divisor, $\ell(D)=\dim H^0(\mathcal{O}(D))$.

Applications: Theta functions appear in string theory partition functions, KdV solitons (finite-gap solutions), number theory (Jacobi's four-square theorem), and cryptography (elliptic curve pairings).

Dessins d'Enfants & Belyi Maps

A Belyi function is a holomorphic map $f:\Sigma\to\mathbb{CP}^1$ ramified only over $\{0,1,\infty\}$.

Dessin d'enfant: the preimage $f^{-1}([0,1])\subset\Sigma$ is a connected bipartite graph embedded on the surface — black vertices at $f^{-1}(0)$, white at $f^{-1}(1)$, edges are preimages of $[0,1]$.

Belyi's theorem (1979): A Riemann surface $\Sigma$ is defined over $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$ if and only if it admits a Belyi map.

Grothendieck's "Esquisse d'un Programme" (1984): Grothendieck was stunned: a purely combinatorial object (the graph) encodes the algebraic structure over $\bar{\mathbb{Q}}$.

Absolute Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\bar{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ acts faithfully on dessins by permuting the algebraic structures. This gives a combinatorial way to study the most mysterious group in mathematics.

Passport of a dessin: the triple of cycle types $(\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_\infty)$ of the monodromy permutations at $0,1,\infty$. Each is a partition of $\deg f$.

Modular curves $X(N)$ and $X_0(N)$ are natural examples: the $j$-function gives a Belyi map $X(1)\to\mathbb{CP}^1$ ramified at $j=0,1728,\infty$.

Triangle groups: For Belyi maps from $\mathbb{CP}^1$ with passport $([a],[b],[c])$, the monodromy group is a quotient of the $(a,b,c)$-triangle group $\Delta(a,b,c)=\langle x,y,z\,|\,x^a=y^b=z^c=xyz=1\rangle$.

Seiberg-Witten Theory

The Seiberg-Witten curve encodes the exact low-energy effective action of $\mathcal{N}=2$ $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ super-Yang-Mills theory.

SW curve (genus 1): $$\Sigma_{SW}:\quad y^2 = (x^2-\Lambda^4)(x-u)$$ equivalently $y^2=(x-e_1)(x-e_2)(x-e_3)$ with $e_i$ the branch points.

Singular points at $u=\pm\Lambda^2$: the curve degenerates (a cycle pinches), a BPS state becomes massless.

SW differential: $\lambda_{SW} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4\pi}\frac{x\,dx}{y}$ — a meromorphic 1-form on $\Sigma_{SW}$.

Period integrals: $$a = \oint_A \lambda_{SW},\qquad a_D = \oint_B \lambda_{SW}$$ $a$ is the scalar vev (Coulomb branch coordinate); $a_D=\partial\mathcal{F}/\partial a$ is its dual. The prepotential $\mathcal{F}(a)$ encodes all perturbative and instanton corrections.

Electric-magnetic duality: Under $\tau_{SW}=\partial^2\mathcal{F}/\partial a^2\in\mathbb{H}$, the duality group $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ acts on $(a,a_D)$. This is exact S-duality.

Revolution (1994): Seiberg & Witten solved the exact quantum moduli space — the first non-perturbative exact result in 4d QFT. The discriminant locus $\Delta=0$ maps out where the physics is singular (massless monopoles/dyons).

Donaldson invariants of 4-manifolds are computed by the SW invariants — a profound link between physics and topology (Witten 1994).

Spectral Curves & Integrable Systems

The spectral curve of a Hitchin system on a curve $C$ is defined by $$\Sigma_{spec}=\{(x,y)\in T^*C : \det(y\cdot\mathrm{Id} - \Phi(x))=0\}$$ where $\Phi$ is a Higgs field (meromorphic section of $\mathrm{End}(E)\otimes K_C$).

For $\mathrm{GL}_2$ on $\mathbb{CP}^1$ with poles at $p_1,\ldots,p_n$: the spectral curve is a degree-2 branched cover $\Sigma\to\mathbb{CP}^1$, given by $$y^2 = \det\Phi = P_{2g+2}(x)$$ a hyperelliptic curve of genus $g$.

Lax pair: $L(x,t),\,M(x,t)$ with $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial t} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial x} + [L,M]=0$$ This zero-curvature equation encodes isomonodromic deformation and Liouville integrability.

Liouville integrability: Hitchin Hamiltonians $H_i=\oint\mathrm{tr}\,\Phi^i$ Poisson-commute. Action-angle variables are given by the Jacobian of $\Sigma_{spec}$.

KdV equation $u_t=6uu_x-u_{xxx}$ as Hitchin system: the finite-gap solutions $u(x,t)=2\partial_x^2\log\theta(\mathbf{U}x+\mathbf{V}t+\mathbf{W}|\Omega)$ are governed by the spectral curve.

Gaiotto theories (2009): 4d $\mathcal{N}=2$ SCFTs of class $\mathcal{S}$ arise from wrapping M5-branes on $C$ with punctures. The Seiberg-Witten curve IS the spectral curve of the associated Hitchin system.

Geometric Langlands: the Hitchin fibration $h:\mathcal{M}_{Higgs}\to\mathcal{A}$ (projection to spectral data) is a crucial ingredient in the geometric Langlands correspondence and mirror symmetry $(\mathcal{M}_{Higgs}(G)\leftrightarrow\mathcal{M}_{Higgs}(^LG))$.
