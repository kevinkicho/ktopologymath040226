What is a rotation group?
SO(n) is the group of n×n orthogonal matrices with determinant +1. Each element represents a rotation that preserves orientation and distance. These groups are Lie groups — continuous groups that are also smooth manifolds.
SO(2) ≅ U(1) ≅ S¹
Every 2D rotation is determined by a single angle θ. The group manifold is the circle S¹. It is abelian: R(α)R(β) = R(α+β).

Physical relevance: SO(2) appears in pendulum phase spaces, polarizer optics, and as the gauge group U(1) of electromagnetism in the Standard Model.
SO(3) ≅ RP³
3D rotations require 3 parameters (Euler angles, or axis+angle). The manifold is RP³ — a 3-ball of radius π with antipodal boundary points identified.

Key fact: SO(3) is not simply connected. A 360° rotation cannot be shrunk to a point, but a 720° rotation can. This is why spinors (SU(2) double cover) are needed in quantum mechanics.
Why rotation groups matter: Angular momentum in quantum mechanics, rigid body dynamics in robotics, molecular symmetry in chemistry, and gauge symmetry in particle physics all depend on SO(n). The topology of these groups determines what kinds of particles can exist.

What You're Seeing
A plate is connected to a wall by elastic belts. When the plate rotates 360°, the belts become tangled — they cannot unwind without moving the plate.

But at 720°, the belts return to flat! This demonstrates a fundamental topological property: a 360° loop in SO(3) is not contractible, but a 720° loop is.
Why 720° and not 360°?
SO(3) has fundamental group π₁ ≅ &ZZ;₂. A rotation by 360° traces a non-contractible loop in SO(3) — you can't deform it back to the identity without leaving the group. But 720° traces a contractible loop — it can be smoothly shrunk to a point.
The physical consequence
Objects with half-integer spin (fermions: electrons, quarks, neutrinos) transform under SU(2), the double cover of SO(3). A 360° rotation multiplies the wavefunction by −1. Only a 720° rotation returns it to +1.

This was verified experimentally: neutron interferometry (Werner et al., 1975) directly observed the −1 phase shift after a 360° rotation.
Try it yourself: Hold a cup of water in your hand. Rotate it 360° under your arm — your arm is now twisted. Continue another 360° in the same direction, and your arm untwists. That's the Dirac belt trick, and it proves SO(3) ≠ simply connected!

SU(2) ≅ S³
Every 2×2 unitary matrix with det = 1:
$U = \begin{pmatrix}\alpha & -\bar\beta \\ \beta & \bar\alpha\end{pmatrix}$
satisfies $|\alpha|^2+|\beta|^2=1$,
which is exactly the 3-sphere $S^3 \subset \mathbb{R}^4$.
SO(3) ≅ RP³ = S³/ℤ₂
Antipodal points $U$ and $-U$ on S³ map to the same rotation. So SO(3) is the 3-sphere with antipodal identifications.

π₁(SO(3)) = ℤ₂ — one non-contractible loop class.
π₁(SU(2)) = 0 — simply connected.

SU(3) / A₂ Root System
Rank 2, dimension 8.
6 root vectors + 2 Cartan generators = 8 generators total (Gell-Mann matrices).

Simple roots: α₁, α₂
Positive roots: α₁, α₂, α₁+α₂
Weyl group = S₃ (symmetric group, order 6).
Adjoint action
Cartan generators $H_i$:
$[H_i, E_\alpha] = \alpha_i\, E_\alpha$
Roots $\alpha$ are eigenvalues of ad($H_i$).

Root lengths: all roots in A₂ have equal length; the angle between $\alpha_1$ and $\alpha_2$ is 120°.
8-dimensional Adjoint Rep
The adjoint representation has weights = the 6 roots plus the 2 zero weights (Cartan generators). This is the octet of QCD — the 8 gluons!
Weyl reflections
Each root $\alpha$ defines a reflection hyperplane $H_\alpha$ (perpendicular bisector through origin). The Weyl group acts by reflecting roots across these planes, permuting the root system.

Vector vs Spinor
A 3D vector (spin-1) returns to itself after $\theta = 2\pi$.

A spinor (spin-½) acquires phase $e^{i\theta/2}$, so at $\theta = 2\pi$ it becomes $-|\psi\rangle$. It needs $\theta = 4\pi$ to return to $+|\psi\rangle$.
D½ under composition
$D^{1/2}(R_1 R_2) = D^{1/2}(R_1)\,D^{1/2}(R_2)$

This is a projective representation of SO(3) — it satisfies the group law only up to a sign $\pm 1$. It is an exact representation of SU(2).
Physical consequence
The Aharonov–Susskind experiment (1967) and neutron interferometry (Werner et al., 1975; Rauch et al., 1975) directly confirmed the $4\pi$ periodicity of spinors via quantum interference. Half-integer spin particles (fermions) live in the double cover SU(2).
Key formula: For rotation by $\theta$ around $\hat{z}$:
$D^{1/2}(R_z(\theta)) = \begin{pmatrix} e^{-i\theta/2} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{+i\theta/2} \end{pmatrix}$

At $\theta=2\pi$: $D^{1/2} = -I$. At $\theta=4\pi$: $D^{1/2} = +I$.
